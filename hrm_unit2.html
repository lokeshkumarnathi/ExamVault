1. Basics of Job Analysis

Topic 01
Definition: Job Analysis is the systematic process of collecting and analysing information about the duties, responsibilities, skills, and working conditions of a specific job.

Purpose of Job analysis

  • Foundation for all HR activities — recruitment, training, appraisal, compensation
  • Helps write accurate Job Descriptions and Job Specifications
  • Clarifies roles, avoids duplication
  • Basis for legal EEO compliance

Outcomes of Job Analysis

Job Description

What the job involves — duties, responsibilities, reporting lines, working conditions

Job Specification

What the person needs — qualifications, experience, skills, personality traits

Steps in Job Analysis

1. Identify purpose→ 2. Review background info→ 3. Select representative jobs→ 4. Collect data→ 5. Verify data→ 6. Prepare JD & JS

2. Talent Management Process

Topic 02
Definition: Talent Management is the integrated process of attracting, identifying, developing, retaining, and deploying high-potential employees to meet current and future organisational needs.

Talent Management Process (7 Steps)

Plan→ Attract→ Select→ Develop→ Retain→ Deploy→ Transition

Components

  • Workforce planning — identifying future talent needs
  • Recruiting — sourcing candidates from inside and outside
  • Onboarding — integrating new hires effectively
  • Learning & Development — building skills
  • Performance Management — evaluating contribution
  • Succession Planning — pipeline of future leaders
  • Retention — keeping top performers engaged

Importance

  • Ensures right person in right role at right time
  • Builds competitive advantage through people
  • Reduces turnover cost
  • Supports business strategy

Real-World Example: Wipro's "Talent Transformation" program identifies high-potential employees early, gives them accelerated learning paths and global assignments — classic talent management.

Topic 04

Contents of JD

  • Department and location
  • Summary of the job
  • Duties and responsibilities
  • Reporting structure
  • Working conditions
  • Equipment / tools used

Contents of JS

  • Educational qualifications
  • Experience required
  • Skills and competencies
  • Physical requirements
  • Personality traits
  • Emotional attributes

5. Job Satisfaction

Topic 05
Definition: Job Satisfaction is the positive emotional state or pleasurable feeling that results from the appraisal of one's job or job experiences (Locke, 1976).

Factors influencing Job Satisfaction

Intrinsic factors

  • Nature of work
  • Achievement
  • Recognition
  • Growth opportunities

Extrinsic factors

  • Pay and benefits
  • Working conditions
  • Supervision quality
  • Co-worker relationships

Theories related to Job Satisfaction

  • Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory: Hygiene factors (prevent dissatisfaction) + Motivators (create satisfaction)
  • Maslow's Hierarchy: Higher needs (esteem, self-actualisation) create job satisfaction
  • Hackman & Oldham's Job Characteristics Model: Skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, feedback → satisfaction

Consequences of Job Satisfaction / Dissatisfaction

High SatisfactionLow Satisfaction
High productivityAbsenteeism
Low turnoverHigh turnover
Better customer serviceGrievances & conflicts
Innovation and creativityPoor performance

Measurement: Job Descriptive Index (JDI), Minnesota Satisfaction Questionnaire (MSQ)

6. Job Enlargement

Topic 06
Definition: Job Enlargement is the horizontal expansion of a job by adding more tasks of a similar nature and skill level, without increasing authority or responsibility.

Key Features

  • Horizontal expansion (more tasks, same level)
  • Reduces monotony and boredom
  • Does NOT add more responsibility or authority
  • Also called "job widening" or "horizontal loading"

Advantages

  • Reduces monotony in routine jobs
  • Employee learns multiple skills
  • Greater flexibility in workforce

Disadvantages

  • Not truly motivating — "more of the same"
  • May cause fatigue if too many tasks added
  • Does not address deeper dissatisfaction

Example: A bank teller who only handles deposits is also given the task of processing withdrawals and currency exchange — same level, more variety.

Memory Trick: Enlargement = Wider (horizontal). Think of a river getting WIDER but not DEEPER.
Horizontal LoadingJob WideningVarietyMonotony Reduction

7. Job Enrichment

Topic 07
Definition: Job Enrichment is the vertical expansion of a job by adding more responsibility, authority, decision-making power, and opportunities for growth (based on Herzberg's motivators).

Key Features

  • Vertical expansion (more authority, higher-level tasks)
  • Based on Herzberg's Motivation-Hygiene Theory
  • Also called "vertical loading"
  • Gives employees control over planning, executing, and evaluating work

Techniques of Job Enrichment

  • Giving direct feedback to employees
  • Allowing employees to set their own schedules
  • Assigning complete units of work (not fragmented tasks)
  • Increasing accountability
  • Providing opportunities for personal growth

Advantages vs. Disadvantages

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Higher motivation and satisfactionNot suitable for all employees
Develops managerial talentMay increase anxiety in some
Reduces absenteeism and turnoverCostly to implement
Builds employee competenceNeeds redesigning of entire job

Example: A sales executive is given the authority to approve small discounts, prepare client proposals, and manage their own client portfolio instead of just making calls.

Memory Trick: Enrichment = Deeper (vertical). Think of a river getting DEEPER and more meaningful.
Vertical LoadingHerzbergAutonomyResponsibilityMotivation

8. Job Rotation

Topic 08
Definition: Job Rotation is the systematic movement of employees from one job to another within the organisation at planned intervals to broaden their experience and reduce monotony.

Key Features

  • Lateral movement across different jobs/departments
  • Planned and systematic — not random transfers
  • Develops multi-skilled, versatile employees
  • Used extensively in management trainee programs

Types of Job Rotation

  • Within a department: e.g., accounts clerk moves from billing to payroll
  • Across departments: e.g., marketing trainee works in sales, then operations
  • Cross-functional: e.g., engineer spends 6 months in customer service

Advantages vs. Disadvantages

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Develops generalist managersTemporary loss of productivity
Reduces boredom and monotonyTraining costs increase
Succession planning supportEmployees may resist rotation
Better team understandingLoss of specialisation

Example: In Hindustan Unilever's management trainee program, graduates rotate through sales, supply chain, marketing, and finance over 18 months before taking a final posting.

10. Recruitment Process

Topic 10
Definition: Recruitment is the process of searching for and attracting a pool of qualified candidates from which suitable candidates can be selected for available job vacancies.

Recruitment Process Steps

1. Identify vacancy→ 2. Job Analysis→ 3. Prepare JD & JS→ 4. Choose source→ 5. Advertise→ 6. Receive applications→ 7. Screening→ 8. Pass to Selection

Factors affecting Recruitment

Internal Factors

  • Organisational size
  • Recruitment policy
  • Image/brand
  • Budget available

External Factors

  • Labour market conditions
  • Government regulations
  • Competitors' HR practices
  • Economic conditions

11. Selection Process

Topic 11
Definition: Selection is the process of choosing the most suitable candidate from the pool of applicants by eliminating those who do not meet the required qualifications and criteria.

Selection Process Steps

1. Application screening→ 2. Preliminary interview→ 3. Written tests→ 4. Employment interview→ 5. Medical exam→ 6. Reference check→ 7. Final approval→ 8. Job offer

Key concept — Selection Ratio: Number of candidates selected ÷ Total applicants. Lower ratio = more selective = more rigorous process.

14. Recruitment of Diverse Workforce

Topic 14
Definition: Diversity recruitment is the intentional effort to attract, hire, and retain employees from a variety of backgrounds — including gender, age, ethnicity, religion, disability, and sexual orientation.

Why Diverse Recruitment Matters

  • Brings diverse perspectives → innovation and creativity
  • Reflects customer diversity → better market understanding
  • Legal compliance with EEO and affirmative action
  • Builds employer brand and reputation

Strategies for Diverse Recruitment

  • Bias-free job descriptions (gender-neutral language)
  • Partnering with women's colleges, SC/ST NGOs, disability organisations
  • Blind resume screening (removing names, gender, photos)
  • Diverse interview panels
  • Flexible work options to attract caregivers
  • Internship programs targeting underrepresented groups

Example: Accenture India publicly commits to achieving 50% gender parity and runs specific recruitment drives for women returning to work ("Back to Work" program).

Errors in Interviews

Topic 17 — Very Important

Interview errors are biases that distort an interviewer's judgement, leading to incorrect hiring decisions. Knowing and avoiding them improves selection quality.

1. Halo Effect

The interviewer allows one positive trait to overshadow all other attributes. A well-dressed candidate is assumed to be competent, hardworking, and intelligent — without proper evidence.

Example: A good-looking, confident candidate is rated highly even though their technical skills are weak.

2. Horn Effect (Reverse Halo)

One negative trait creates an overall negative impression. The opposite of the Halo Effect.

Example: A candidate who arrives 5 minutes late is judged as irresponsible and disorganised throughout the interview.

3. First Impression Error (Primacy Effect)

The interviewer forms a judgement in the first 3–5 minutes and spends the rest of the interview confirming it. Early positive impression = favourable rating; early negative = unfavourable.

Example: A candidate gives a strong confident handshake and introduction, and the interviewer positively interprets all subsequent answers.

4. Contrast Error

Rating a candidate relative to the previous candidate rather than against the job standard. A good candidate after several poor ones seems excellent; a good candidate after an exceptional one seems average.

Example: Candidate 4 is only average but after three very poor candidates, they seem outstanding.

5. Similarity Bias (Similar-to-Me Effect)

Interviewers rate candidates higher if they share similar backgrounds, hobbies, schools, or interests — regardless of job-related qualifications.

Example: An interviewer from IIT rates an IIT alumnus candidate higher than a better-qualified candidate from another college.

6. Non-verbal Bias

Judgements made based on body language, eye contact, tone of voice, or physical appearance rather than actual competence.

Example: A highly capable introvert who does not make direct eye contact is rated poorly while an extrovert with weaker skills is rated highly.

7. Stereotyping

Making generalisations about a group and applying them to an individual — based on gender, age, caste, religion, or region.

Example: "Women are not suitable for field sales roles" — rejecting a highly qualified female candidate based on this assumption.

8. Central Tendency Error

Rating all candidates as "average" to avoid controversy, resulting in ratings clustered around the midpoint and no differentiation.

9. Leniency / Strictness Error

Leniency: Rating everyone too positively. Strictness: Rating everyone too negatively. Both prevent accurate differentiation.

10. Recency Effect

Last-heard information is weighted more than earlier information. The interviewer remembers the end of the conversation most vividly.

⭐ Exam Point: Halo Effect, Horn Effect, Contrast Error, First Impression, and Similarity Bias are the 5 most frequently asked interview errors. Know definitions AND examples for each.
Memory Trick (6 main errors): HH-FCS-N — Halo, Horn, First impression, Contrast, Similarity, Non-verbal bias

How to Reduce Interview Errors

  • Use structured interviews with standardised questions
  • Train interviewers in bias awareness
  • Use panel interviews (multiple perspectives)
  • Use scoring rubrics and rating scales
  • Focus strictly on job-related criteria
  • Record interviews (where legally permitted)

Exam Questions & Model Answers

2-Mark Questions

Define Job Analysis.2 marks
Job Analysis is the systematic process of collecting, analysing, and interpreting information about the duties, responsibilities, skills required, and working conditions of a job. It results in two key documents: the Job Description and Job Specification.
What is Job Enrichment?2 marks
Job Enrichment is the vertical expansion of a job by adding more authority, responsibility, and decision-making opportunities to an existing job. It is based on Herzberg's Motivation-Hygiene Theory and is also called vertical loading. Example: A sales executive who is also given authority to approve discounts and manage client portfolios.
What is the Halo Effect in interviews?2 marks
The Halo Effect is an interview error where one positive quality of a candidate influences the interviewer to rate all other qualities positively as well. For example, a well-dressed and confident candidate may be rated highly on technical skills even without adequate evidence.
Distinguish between Recruitment and Selection in one sentence each.2 marks
Recruitment is a positive process of searching for and attracting a pool of qualified candidates to apply for a job vacancy. Selection is a negative process of evaluating and eliminating candidates to identify the single most suitable person for the job.
What is a Job Specification?2 marks
A Job Specification is a written statement describing the minimum qualifications, skills, education, experience, and personal attributes that a candidate must possess to perform the job successfully. It focuses on the person, while the Job Description focuses on the job.
What is Talent Management?2 marks
Talent Management is the integrated HR process of attracting, identifying, developing, engaging, and retaining high-potential employees to meet current and future organisational goals. It includes workforce planning, succession planning, L&D, and retention strategies.
What is HRP (Human Resource Planning)?2 marks
HRP is the process of forecasting an organisation's future HR needs (demand) and ensuring that the right number of people with the right skills are available at the right time. It involves both demand forecasting and supply forecasting to identify and fill HR gaps.

5-Mark Questions

Differentiate between Job Enlargement, Job Enrichment, and Job Rotation with examples.5 marks
Job Enlargement is the horizontal expansion of a job by adding more tasks of similar nature and skill level. It reduces monotony but does not add responsibility. Example: A bank teller who handles deposits is also assigned withdrawals and currency exchange.

Job Enrichment is the vertical expansion of a job by adding greater authority, responsibility, and decision-making power. Based on Herzberg's theory. It creates intrinsic motivation. Example: A sales executive given authority to approve discounts and manage their own client portfolio.

Job Rotation is the systematic lateral movement of employees across different jobs or departments at planned intervals. It develops multi-skilled employees. Example: Management trainees at HUL rotate across marketing, sales, and supply chain for 18 months.

Key Differences:
Direction: Enlargement = Horizontal | Enrichment = Vertical | Rotation = Lateral
Change in authority: Enlargement = No | Enrichment = Yes | Rotation = No
Motivation level: Enrichment is highest because it addresses Herzberg's motivators.
Skill development: Rotation develops the broadest range of skills.
Explain any five types of interviews used in the selection process.5 marks
1. Structured Interview: Uses pre-planned, standardised questions asked to all candidates in the same order. Scores are based on a rating rubric. It has the highest predictive validity and is legally defensible.

2. Unstructured Interview: A free-flowing conversation without fixed questions. The interviewer adapts based on responses. Good for assessing personality but highly subjective.

3. Behavioural Interview (BEI): Based on the principle that past behaviour predicts future performance. Uses the STAR method — Situation, Task, Action, Result. Most commonly used by MNCs today.

4. Stress Interview: Deliberately creates pressure through challenging, aggressive, or unexpected questions to observe how the candidate reacts under stress. Used for high-pressure roles.

5. Panel Interview: Multiple interviewers (typically 3–5) simultaneously assess one candidate. Reduces individual bias and provides a more balanced assessment. Common in government and senior executive selection.
Explain the sources of recruitment — internal and external.5 marks
Introduction: Sources of recruitment are the channels through which organisations attract candidates for job vacancies. They are broadly classified into internal and external sources.

Internal Sources:
1. Promotion — moving deserving employees to higher positions
2. Transfer — lateral movement to fill vacancies in another department
3. Job Posting — internally advertising vacancies on notice boards or intranet
4. Employee Referrals — existing employees recommend suitable candidates
5. Rehiring — re-employing former employees (boomerang hires)

Advantages of internal recruitment: Low cost, fast, boosts employee morale, candidate knows the culture.

External Sources:
1. Campus Recruitment — hiring fresh graduates from colleges
2. Job Portals — Naukri.com, LinkedIn, Indeed
3. Employment Agencies — TeamLease, Randstad
4. Advertisements — digital, newspaper, social media
5. Headhunting — executive search firms for senior roles

Advantages of external recruitment: Fresh perspectives, larger talent pool, new skills not available internally.

Conclusion: A balanced mix of internal and external sources is most effective. Internal sources maintain morale; external sources bring innovation and fresh talent.
Explain the errors/biases that occur in interviews and how to overcome them.5 marks
Introduction: Interview errors are systematic biases that affect an interviewer's judgement, leading to poor hiring decisions. The major errors are:

1. Halo Effect: One good quality influences overall positive rating. Solution: Focus on each dimension separately.

2. Horn Effect: One negative trait creates overall negative impression. Solution: Structured evaluation forms.

3. First Impression Error: Decision made in first 3–5 minutes and confirmed thereafter. Solution: Delay finalising assessment until the end.

4. Contrast Error: Candidate rated relative to previous candidate rather than job standard. Solution: Evaluate each candidate against the job specification, not against each other.

5. Similarity Bias: Preference for candidates who resemble the interviewer in background or interests. Solution: Diverse panel interviews, blind screening.

6. Non-verbal Bias: Judgements based on appearance, eye contact, or tone rather than content. Solution: Use structured scoring rubrics focused on answers.

How to reduce errors: Use structured interviews, train interviewers, use panel interviews, use rating scales anchored to job criteria, separate scoring from selection decision.

10-Mark Essay Questions

Explain the Recruitment and Selection process in detail. How do they differ?10 marks
Introduction: Recruitment and Selection are two sequential but distinct HR processes for filling job vacancies with the right candidates. Together they form the "staffing" function of HRM.

RECRUITMENT
Definition: Recruitment is the process of searching for and attracting a pool of qualified candidates from which the most suitable can be selected.

Nature: It is a positive process — it encourages as many suitable candidates as possible to apply.

Process of Recruitment:
1. Identify the vacancy (due to resignation, expansion, or new role)
2. Conduct Job Analysis → prepare JD and JS
3. Choose appropriate source (internal or external)
4. Create and publish job advertisement
5. Receive and acknowledge applications
6. Initial screening of applications
7. Shortlisting candidates for selection process

Sources: Internal (promotion, transfer, job posting, referrals) and External (campus, portals, agencies, ads, headhunting).

SELECTION
Definition: Selection is the process of evaluating and choosing the most suitable candidate from the pool of applicants by systematically eliminating unsuitable ones.

Nature: It is a negative process — it eliminates candidates who do not meet the required criteria.

Process of Selection:
1. Application form review and screening
2. Preliminary / telephonic interview
3. Written / psychometric tests
4. Detailed employment interview (structured, panel, behavioural)
5. Background and reference check
6. Medical / physical examination
7. Final selection decision by HR and line manager
8. Job offer letter extended

Key Selection Concepts: Selection Ratio (candidates selected/total applicants), Test Reliability and Validity, Predictive Validity.

Key Differences (Recruitment vs. Selection):
Recruitment = Positive | Selection = Negative
Recruitment = Attract applicants | Selection = Eliminate unsuitable
Recruitment comes first | Selection comes after
Recruitment tools = Ads, portals | Selection tools = Tests, interviews
Recruitment = Lower cost | Selection = Higher cost

Conclusion: Recruitment and selection are complementary processes. Effective recruitment creates a strong talent pool; effective selection ensures the best fit is chosen, reducing turnover and improving organisational performance. Both must be aligned with EEO principles to ensure fairness and legal compliance.
Explain Human Resource Planning — its process, importance, and forecasting methods.10 marks
Introduction: Human Resource Planning (HRP) is the process of systematically forecasting the future supply and demand for human resources in an organisation to ensure that the right people with the right skills are available at the right time. It is the foundation of all HR activities.

Objectives of HRP:
1. Avoid surplus (overstaffing) and shortage (understaffing) of employees
2. Ensure smooth succession of leadership
3. Plan for training and development needs
4. Align HR strategy with business strategy
5. Control manpower costs effectively

HRP Process (6 Steps):
Step 1: Analyse organisational plans and objectives — understand business growth plans, new products, expansions
Step 2: Forecast HR Demand — estimate how many and what type of employees are needed in the future
Step 3: Assess Current HR Supply — analyse current workforce (skills, experience, potential)
Step 4: Identify the HR Gap — compare demand with supply; determine surplus or deficit
Step 5: Develop Action Plans — if deficit: recruit, train, promote; if surplus: redeploy, VRS, retrench
Step 6: Monitor and Review — compare actual vs. planned HR outcomes and revise

Demand Forecasting Methods:
1. Trend Analysis — study past data on employment levels, project future needs
2. Ratio Analysis — relationship between business factor (e.g., sales revenue) and number of employees needed
3. Scatter Plot — graphically plots relationship between activity and staffing level
4. Managerial Judgement — managers at each level estimate their team's future needs (bottom-up: aggregated upward; top-down: senior management estimates, divided downward)
5. Computerised Forecasting — uses regression and other statistical models

Supply Forecasting Methods:
1. Skills Inventory — computerised database of every employee's skills, education, and work history
2. Replacement Charts — show who is ready to replace each key position holder; highlights readiness levels
3. Succession Planning — identifying high-potential individuals and developing them for future senior roles
4. Markov Analysis — tracks historical movement patterns (promotion, lateral, exit rates) to predict future supply

Importance of HRP:
1. Prevents unexpected talent shortages during business growth
2. Reduces costs from last-minute hiring
3. Ensures training is planned in advance
4. Supports diversity and EEO planning
5. Facilitates international expansion planning

Conclusion: HRP is the backbone of strategic HRM. Without it, organisations react to HR problems rather than anticipating them. In today's fast-changing environment, organisations like TCS, Infosys, and Reliance invest heavily in HRP to sustain competitive advantage through their people.
Explain the concept of Job Analysis. How is it conducted? What are its uses?10 marks
Introduction: Job Analysis is the systematic process of collecting, analysing, and interpreting information about a job's duties, responsibilities, required skills, outcomes, and working conditions. It is often called the "cornerstone of HRM" because virtually every HR function depends on it.

Definition (Edwin Flippo): "Job Analysis is the process of studying and collecting information relating to the operations and responsibilities of a specific job."

Purpose of Job Analysis:
Job Analysis produces two key outputs:
1. Job Description (JD) — what the job involves
2. Job Specification (JS) — what the person needs

Process of Job Analysis:
Step 1: Identify the purpose — why is the job analysis being done? (Recruitment? Appraisal? Compensation?)
Step 2: Review existing information — review org charts, previous JDs, industry benchmarks
Step 3: Select representative positions — analyse a sample of similar jobs rather than all
Step 4: Collect job data — using interviews, questionnaires, observation, diaries, or PAQ
Step 5: Verify information — with job holder and supervisor to ensure accuracy
Step 6: Prepare JD and JS — final written documents used in HR decisions

Methods of Collecting Job Analysis Information:
1. Interview — direct conversation with job holders and supervisors
2. Observation — watching employees perform their job
3. Questionnaire — structured forms (e.g., PAQ — Position Analysis Questionnaire with 194 items)
4. Diary / Work Log — employees record their daily activities
5. Technical Conference — panel of subject matter experts discuss job requirements

Uses / Importance of Job Analysis:
1. Recruitment — JD and JS form the basis of job advertisements and selection criteria
2. Selection — tests and interview questions are designed based on job requirements
3. Training — identifies skills gaps and training needs
4. Performance Appraisal — sets performance standards based on job duties
5. Compensation — job evaluation for fair pay relies on job analysis data
6. Job Design — used to redesign jobs (enlargement, enrichment, rotation)
7. Legal Compliance — ensures selection criteria are job-related (EEO)
8. Career Planning — helps employees understand growth paths

Conclusion: Job Analysis is the foundation of the entire HR system. Without accurate job analysis, organisations cannot recruit effectively, train meaningfully, appraise fairly, or compensate equitably. It is an investment that pays dividends across all HR functions.

Quick Revision Cards — All Topics

Job Analysis

Systematic process of collecting info about a job. Output = JD (what the job is) + JS (who should do it). Methods = IQODT. Foundation of all HR functions.

Talent Management

Attracting, developing, retaining high-potential employees. 7 steps: Plan → Attract → Select → Develop → Retain → Deploy → Transition. Used by Wipro, TCS.

JD vs JS

JD = Job profile (duties, conditions). JS = Candidate profile (education, skills). JD answers WHAT. JS answers WHO. Both derived from Job Analysis.

Job Satisfaction

Positive emotional state from one's job experience (Locke). Influenced by intrinsic (nature of work, growth) and extrinsic (pay, supervision) factors. Measured by JDI, MSQ.

Job Enlargement

HORIZONTAL expansion. More tasks, same level. Reduces monotony. No new authority. Also called job widening / horizontal loading. River gets WIDER.

Job Enrichment

VERTICAL expansion. More authority, responsibility, decision-making. Based on Herzberg. Highest motivation. Also called vertical loading. River gets DEEPER.

Job Rotation

LATERAL movement across jobs/departments. Develops multi-skilled employees. Used in management trainee programs. HUL example. River changes COURSE.

HRP

Right people, right skills, right place, right time. Process: Org plan → Demand forecast → Supply assess → Gap → Action → Monitor. Demand methods: Trend, Ratio, Scatter. Supply: Skills inventory, Markov, Succession.

Recruitment

POSITIVE process. Attracting applicants. Sources: Internal (PTEJR) + External (CJEAWH). Comes before selection. Lower cost. Tools: Ads, portals, campus.

Selection

NEGATIVE process. Choosing best candidate. Steps: APWEMRFO. Selection ratio = selected/total. Key: Reliability + Validity of tests.

Sources of Recruitment

Internal: Promotion, Transfer, Referrals, Job posting, Rehiring. External: Campus, Portals, Agencies, Ads, Walk-in, Headhunting. Internal = cheaper, faster. External = fresher, wider pool.

Employee Testing

Types: Aptitude, Achievement, Personality (OCEAN), Interest, SJT, Psychometric. Key concepts: Reliability (consistency) + Validity (accuracy) + Predictive Validity.

Interview Types

Structured (standardised, most valid), Unstructured (free-flowing), Behavioural (STAR method), Panel (multiple interviewers), Stress (pressure-testing), Situational (hypothetical scenarios), Group.

Interview Errors

HH-FCS-N: Halo (one good → all good), Horn (one bad → all bad), First Impression (first 3 min decides), Contrast (compared to prev candidate), Similarity (like-me bias), Non-verbal bias. Reduce with structured interviews + training.

Diverse Workforce Recruitment

Bias-free JDs, blind screening, diverse panels, partnerships with NGOs and women's colleges, flexible work options. Accenture example — 50% gender parity goal.

HR Forecasting Methods

Demand: Trend analysis, Ratio analysis, Scatter plot, Managerial judgement, Regression. Supply: Skills inventory, Replacement charts, Succession planning, Markov analysis.
