Training, Development & Performance Management

1 — Training & Development: Foundations

University Exam Definition — Training

Training is a short-term, systematic process of improving the knowledge, skills, attitudes, and behaviour of employees for performing a specific job effectively. It is job-oriented and focuses on present needs.

University Exam Definition — Development

Development is a long-term educational process aimed at preparing an individual for broader responsibilities in the future. It is person-oriented and focuses on future needs, personality, and potential.

Importance of Training and Development

For the Organisation

  • Increases productivity and efficiency
  • Reduces accidents and errors
  • Reduces employee turnover and absenteeism
  • Improves quality of products/services
  • Helps in organizational growth
  • Reduces supervision costs

For the Employee

  • Increases knowledge and skills
  • Boosts morale and motivation
  • Improves career growth prospects
  • Increases job satisfaction
  • Reduces fear of new technology
  • Enhances self-confidence

The Training Process — Step by Step

The training process follows a structured, logical sequence:

  1. Identifying Training Needs (TNA)
    Analyse gaps between current and required performance at organisational, task, and individual levels.
  2. Setting Training Objectives
    Define SMART goals — what employees should be able to do after training.
  3. Designing the Training Programme
    Decide content, method, duration, trainers, location, and budget.
  4. Implementation
    Deliver the training using selected methods (on-the-job / off-the-job).
  5. Evaluation
    Measure effectiveness using Kirkpatrick's 4-level model or ROI analysis.
Mnemonic — Training Process

"I See Dogs In Every Yard" → Identify → Set objectives → Design → Implement → Evaluate → (Y)ield results

Training Need Analysis (TNA)

Definition

TNA (Training Need Analysis) is the process of identifying the gap between the current performance of employees and the desired performance standard, to determine what training is required, for whom, and when.

Levels of TNA

1. Organisational Level

Identifies training needs based on company goals, strategy, and changes in technology or environment.

Example: TCS plans to adopt AI — all IT staff need AI training.

2. Task / Job Level

Identifies skills, knowledge, and abilities required to perform a specific job role effectively.

Example: A sales job requires negotiation skills, product knowledge, CRM tools.

3. Individual / Person Level

Compares actual employee performance with required performance to identify personal training gaps.

Example: Riya's sales targets are missed → needs negotiation training.

Methods of Collecting TNA Data

  • Performance Appraisals — reveal gaps in individual performance
  • Observations — direct watching of employees at work
  • Interviews — one-on-one discussions with employees and managers
  • Questionnaires / Surveys — structured data collection
  • Exit Interviews — identify why employees leave
  • Tests and Assessments — measure knowledge/skill gaps directly
  • Job Analysis — study of job requirements

Designing the Training Programme

Once needs are identified, the programme is designed considering:

Training = short-term, job-oriented. Development = long-term, person-oriented.

TNA has 3 levels: Organisational → Task → Individual.

Training Process: Identify → Set Objectives → Design → Implement → Evaluate.

OJT = at workplace, low cost, learning by doing. Off-JT = away from work, focused, costly.

2 — Training Methods

Training methods are broadly divided into On-the-Job and Off-the-Job methods.

On-the-Job Training Methods

1. Coaching

Definition

Coaching is a one-on-one training process where a senior/experienced employee (coach) guides a junior employee to improve their performance on specific tasks.

  • Key Feature: Personalised, continuous feedback
  • Advantage: Individual attention, flexible, low cost
  • Disadvantage: Quality depends on coach's ability; coach may not have time
  • Example: A senior sales manager coaching a new sales rep on closing deals at HUL.

2. Mentoring

Definition

Mentoring is a long-term developmental relationship where an experienced mentor provides career guidance, personal support, and wisdom to a less experienced mentee.

  • Key Feature: Focuses on career growth and personality development
  • Difference from Coaching: Coaching = task-focused (short-term); Mentoring = career-focused (long-term)
  • Advantage: Builds confidence, career vision, networking
  • Example: Infosys assigns senior leaders as mentors to high-potential employees.

3. Job Rotation

Definition

Job Rotation is a method where employees are moved between different jobs/departments at regular intervals to gain broad experience across multiple functions.

  • Purpose: Multi-skilling, reduces boredom, prepares for promotion
  • Advantage: Employee learns multiple functions; reduces monotony
  • Disadvantage: Reduces efficiency in each role; may cause frustration
  • Example: Mahindra & Mahindra rotates management trainees across Finance, Marketing, and Operations.

4. Apprenticeship / Internship

Definition

Apprenticeship is a formal training contract where a learner works under a master craftsperson for a period to learn a skilled trade. Internship is a temporary, structured work experience given to students or fresh graduates.

  • Apprenticeship: Used in skilled trades (plumbing, electrical, welding)
  • Internship: Used by companies to develop fresh talent
  • Advantage: Real work experience, earn while learning
  • Example: Wipro's campus internship programme; ITI apprentices in manufacturing firms.

Off-the-Job Training Methods

5. Vestibule Training

Definition

Vestibule Training is a method where employees are trained on equipment/machines that closely replicate the actual work environment, but in a separate training area (vestibule) — not on the actual shop floor.

  • Ideal for: Factory workers, airline pilots, bank tellers
  • Advantage: Safe, no disruption to production, realistic
  • Disadvantage: Expensive to set up duplicate equipment
  • Example: IndiGo airlines trains pilots on full-motion flight simulators before they fly real aircraft.

6. Role Playing

Definition

Role Playing is a training method where trainees enact real-life situations (such as a customer complaint, performance review, or negotiation) to practise human relations and interpersonal skills.

  • Ideal for: Customer service, HR professionals, managers
  • Advantage: Develops empathy, communication, problem-solving
  • Disadvantage: Trainees may not take it seriously; embarrassment
  • Example: HDFC Bank trains customer service staff through simulated angry-customer role-play scenarios.

7. Case Study Method

Definition

The Case Study Method presents trainees with a real or hypothetical business problem and asks them to analyse the situation, identify issues, and suggest solutions — developing analytical and decision-making skills.

  • Ideal for: Management education, MBA programmes
  • Advantage: Develops critical thinking, problem-solving, group discussion skills
  • Disadvantage: May not reflect actual workplace dynamics; time-consuming
  • Example: IIM Ahmedabad extensively uses Harvard case studies in MBA training.

8. Lecture Method

Definition

The Lecture Method is the most traditional training approach where a trainer/instructor delivers information to a group of trainees through oral presentation, often supported by slides or handouts.

  • Advantage: Cost-effective for large groups; structured content delivery
  • Disadvantage: One-way communication; passive learners; no feedback
  • Example: New employee induction programmes at most companies.

9. Simulation Training

Definition

Simulation Training uses artificial environments, computer programs, or physical models that replicate real-world conditions to allow trainees to practise skills in a risk-free setting.

  • Ideal for: Military, aviation, medical, nuclear industries
  • Advantage: Safe, realistic, can repeat as many times as needed
  • Disadvantage: Very expensive; tech-dependent
  • Example: Army uses combat simulators; ISRO uses spacecraft simulators; surgeons train on surgical simulators.

10. E-learning

Definition

E-learning (Electronic Learning) is training delivered through digital technology — internet, computer-based programmes, mobile apps, or Learning Management Systems (LMS) — allowing self-paced, anytime, anywhere learning.

  • Advantage: Flexible, scalable, self-paced, cost-effective for large organisations
  • Disadvantage: Requires tech infrastructure; lacks human interaction; self-discipline needed
  • Types: MOOCs (Coursera, Udemy), LMS platforms (Moodle), webinars, podcasts
  • Example: Accenture trains 500,000+ employees globally using its e-learning platform "Accenture Learning."

3 — Management Development & Evaluation

Management Development

Definition

Management Development is the process by which managers acquire the knowledge, skills, attitudes, and experiences necessary to become effective leaders and handle complex organisational challenges now and in the future.

Objectives of Management Development

  • Prepare managers for higher-level responsibilities
  • Improve decision-making and leadership skills
  • Develop strategic thinking and vision
  • Create a pipeline of future leaders (succession planning)
  • Improve interpersonal and communication skills
  • Build change management capability

Management Development Process

  1. Identify Managerial Talent
    Identify high-potential managers through appraisals, assessment centres, and nominations.
  2. Assess Development Needs
    Identify gaps between current managerial competencies and required future competencies.
  3. Develop Individual Development Plans (IDP)
    Create personalised plans with goals, timelines, activities for each manager.
  4. Implement Development Activities
    Use methods like executive coaching, management education (MBA), seminars, job assignments, action learning.
  5. Evaluate Progress
    Review development against IDP targets; 360-degree feedback from peers, subordinates, superiors.

Evaluation of Training & Development Programmes

Definition

Training Evaluation is the systematic process of measuring the effectiveness and impact of training programmes to determine whether the training has achieved its intended objectives.

Kirkpatrick's Four-Level Model of Training Evaluation

This is the most important and frequently examined model of training evaluation.

L1
Reaction — How do trainees feel about the training?

Measured by: Feedback forms, Smile sheets, Surveys immediately after training.
Example: "Was the trainer clear? Was content useful?"

L2
Learning — What knowledge/skills did they actually gain?

Measured by: Pre-test vs Post-test scores, quizzes, skill assessments.

L3
Behaviour — Are they applying the learning on the job?

Measured by: Performance observation, supervisor reports, 360° feedback after 3-6 months.

L4
Results — What is the business impact?

Measured by: Productivity, quality metrics, reduced errors, revenue increase, lower attrition.

Mnemonic — Kirkpatrick's 4 Levels

"Really Lovely Behaviour Results" → Reaction → Learning → Behaviour → Results

ROI of Training (Level 5 — Jack Phillips Extension)

Beyond Kirkpatrick, Phillips added a 5th level — Return on Investment (ROI):

ROI (%) = [(Benefits of Training − Cost of Training) ÷ Cost of Training] × 100

Example: Training cost ₹1,00,000. Productivity gain = ₹3,00,000. ROI = [(3,00,000 − 1,00,000) ÷ 1,00,000] × 100 = 200%

Methods of Evaluating Training

MethodWhen Used
Pre-test / Post-testBefore and after training — measures learning gain
Feedback FormsImmediately after training — measures reaction
Performance RecordsWeeks/months after — measures behaviour change
360° FeedbackAfter training — multi-source performance feedback
Control Group MethodTrained group vs. untrained group comparison
Cost-Benefit AnalysisCompares cost of training with value of outcomes

Quick Revision — Unit 3

Management Development focuses on preparing managers for future leadership roles.

Kirkpatrick's model: Level 1 = Reaction, 2 = Learning, 3 = Behaviour, 4 = Results.

Phillips added Level 5 = ROI = [(Benefits − Cost) / Cost] × 100.

Evaluation methods: Pre/Post test, feedback forms, performance records, 360° feedback.

4 — Performance Management

University Exam Definition — Performance Management

Performance Management is a continuous, systematic process by which the organisation involves its employees, as individuals and members of groups, in improving organisational effectiveness in the accomplishment of organisational mission and goals.

University Exam Definition — Performance Appraisal

Performance Appraisal is the formal, systematic process of evaluating an employee's job performance and productivity in relation to certain pre-established criteria and organisational objectives.

Mnemonic — Remember the Difference

PM = "Progress Monthly" (continuous, broad, future-focused)
PA = "Past Assessment" (periodic, narrow, past-focused)

Performance Appraisal Process — Step by Step

  1. Establish Performance Standards
    Set clear, measurable standards/benchmarks aligned with organisational goals (using KPIs, KRAs, MBO targets). Example: A sales employee must achieve ₹10 lakh monthly sales.
  2. Communicate Standards to Employees
    Make sure every employee clearly understands the expectations and evaluation criteria before the appraisal period begins.
  3. Measure Actual Performance
    Collect data on how the employee actually performed — through observation, records, reports, and feedback from supervisors and peers.
  4. Compare Actual Performance with Standards
    Identify the gap (positive or negative) between what was expected and what was delivered.
  5. Discuss the Appraisal with the Employee
    Conduct a formal appraisal interview — explain the rating, highlight strengths, discuss areas for improvement. Two-way communication is key.
  6. Initiate Corrective Action
    Based on the appraisal, decide on rewards (promotion, increment, bonus) or corrective measures (training, counselling, warning) for poor performers.
Mnemonic — 6-Step PA Process

"Every Cat Must Compare Daily Correctly"
Establish standards → Communicate → Measure → Compare → Discuss → Correct

Objectives of Performance Appraisal

Administrative Objectives

  • Salary revision / increment decisions
  • Promotion or demotion decisions
  • Transfer decisions
  • Termination or retention
  • Identifying training needs

Developmental Objectives

  • Provide performance feedback
  • Identify employee strengths and weaknesses
  • Plan career development
  • Improve communication between manager and employee
  • Motivate employees

Uses / Importance of Performance Appraisal

  • Compensation decisions — basis for increments, bonuses
  • Promotion decisions — identifies promotion-worthy employees
  • Training needs — reveals performance gaps requiring training
  • Motivation tool — rewards good performers, encourages improvement
  • Employee feedback — helps employees understand where they stand
  • Legal protection — documented evidence for HR decisions
  • Succession planning — identifies future leaders

Quick Revision — Unit 4

Performance Management = continuous, broad, future-focused system.

Performance Appraisal = periodic, narrow, past-focused evaluation tool.

PA Process: Establish → Communicate → Measure → Compare → Discuss → Correct.

Purposes of PA: Salary, Promotion, Training, Motivation, Feedback, Legal, Succession.

5 — Appraisal Techniques & Common Errors

Techniques / Methods of Performance Appraisal

Traditional Methods

1. Ranking Method

Employees are ranked from best to worst based on overall performance. The simplest method.

Types: Simple Ranking (1st, 2nd, 3rd...) | Paired Comparison (compare each employee with every other employee) | Forced Distribution (like a bell curve — 10% excellent, 20% good, 40% average, 20% below average, 10% poor).

Advantages
  • Simple and easy to understand
  • Eliminates central tendency
Disadvantages
  • No absolute standards; subjective
  • Demotivating for those at the bottom

2. Rating Scale Method (Graphic Rating Scale)

Evaluators rate employees on several traits (quantity of work, quality of work, attitude, punctuality, teamwork) using a scale — usually 1–5 or 1–10.

Example:

Trait1 (Poor)2345 (Excellent)
Quality of Work☐☐☑☐☐
Team Work☐☐☐☑☐
Advantages
  • Easy to administer; widely used
  • Allows comparison across employees
Disadvantages
  • Susceptible to rater biases (halo effect)
  • Traits may not be job-relevant

3. Checklist Method

The appraiser is given a list of statements/behaviours and simply checks (✓) those that describe the employee.

Types: Simple Checklist | Weighted Checklist (each item has a different weight based on importance) | Forced Choice Checklist (appraiser must choose between two equally favourable or unfavourable statements).

Advantages
  • Simple and quick
  • Reduces personal bias
Disadvantages
  • HR must interpret; appraiser may not understand weightings
  • Expensive to design

Modern Methods

4. 360-Degree Appraisal

Definition

360-Degree Appraisal is a comprehensive multi-source feedback method where an employee is evaluated by superiors, peers, subordinates, customers, and themselves to give a complete, well-rounded picture of performance.

Superior (Boss)
Employee (Self)
Customer
Peers
Subordinates
Advantages
  • Comprehensive, well-rounded view
  • Reduces single-rater bias
  • Improves self-awareness
Disadvantages
  • Time-consuming and expensive
  • Conflicting feedback may confuse employee
  • Peers may give inflated ratings

Example: Infosys, Wipro, Tata Motors use 360° appraisals for managerial staff.

5. Management by Objectives (MBO)

Definition

MBO (proposed by Peter Drucker) is an appraisal technique where manager and employee jointly set specific, measurable objectives at the beginning of the period, and performance is evaluated against achievement of those objectives at the end of the period.

MBO Process:

  1. Manager and employee jointly set SMART objectives
  2. Employee works towards achieving those objectives
  3. Progress is monitored periodically
  4. At the end of period, performance is evaluated against agreed objectives
  5. New objectives set for next period
Advantages
  • Objective, transparent, measurable
  • Employee ownership and motivation
  • Aligns individual goals with org goals
Disadvantages
  • Time-consuming to set objectives
  • Difficult for qualitative roles
  • May ignore team collaboration

Example: Google's OKR (Objectives and Key Results) system is an advanced form of MBO.

6. BARS (Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scale)

Definition

BARS combines the best elements of narrative critical incidents and quantified rating scales. Each point on the rating scale is anchored by specific behavioural examples (critical incidents) that describe what that level of performance actually looks like.

Example — Customer Service Representative, "Handles Complaints":

RatingBehavioural Anchor
5 (Excellent)Calmly listens to customer, apologises, offers immediate solution, and follows up within 24 hours
3 (Average)Listens to customer, offers standard solution from manual
1 (Poor)Becomes defensive, argues with customer, no solution offered
Advantages
  • Very specific and accurate
  • Reduces rater bias
  • Fair and job-relevant
Disadvantages
  • Expensive and time-consuming to develop
  • Needs separate scale for each job

Common Errors / Biases in Performance Appraisal

Appraisal errors occur when raters make mistakes in judging employee performance. These are frequently asked in exams.

Halo Effect

Rating an employee high on ALL dimensions because of one outstanding positive quality. Example: "Rajan is always punctual, so he must be good at everything."

Horn Effect

Opposite of halo — rating an employee low on ALL dimensions because of one negative trait. Example: "Priya missed one deadline, so she must be incompetent overall."

Central Tendency

Rater gives everyone an average rating (3 out of 5) to avoid conflict and controversy. This does not differentiate good and poor performers.

Leniency Error

Rater gives everyone very high ratings to avoid confrontation or appear liked. Everyone seems excellent even if they are not.

Strictness Error

Opposite of leniency — rater gives everyone low ratings even for good performance. Creates demotivation and unfairness.

Recency Error

Rating based only on recent performance (last few weeks) rather than the entire appraisal period. One recent good or bad incident dominates the rating.

Similarity Bias

Rater gives higher ratings to employees who are similar to themselves in background, personality, interests, or alma mater. "He went to the same college as me — must be good."

Personal Bias / Prejudice

Rating influenced by personal feelings about the employee's race, gender, age, religion, etc. — not actual performance.

Mnemonic — Remember Appraisal Errors

"Happy Horses Can't Learn, So Rest Softly, Peacefully"
Halo · Horn · Central tendency · Leniency · Strictness · Recency · Similarity · Personal bias

How to Reduce Appraisal Errors

  • Train appraisers on common biases
  • Use structured, objective methods (BARS, MBO)
  • Use multiple raters (360° appraisal)
  • Maintain critical incident diary throughout the year
  • Set clear, measurable criteria before appraisal

BARS = behaviour-anchored scale, most accurate but most expensive to develop.

Halo (one positive → all high) vs Horn (one negative → all low). Recency = recent events dominate.

6 — Career Management

Definition — Career Management

Career Management is an ongoing process through which individuals plan, develop, and monitor their career progression, while organisations support and guide employees in aligning their career goals with organisational needs.

Components of Career Management

1. Career Planning

Definition

Career Planning is the process by which an individual sets career goals and identifies the steps, paths, and strategies needed to achieve those goals. It is a self-analysis and goal-setting process.

Career Planning Process

  1. Self-Assessment
    Identify your interests, values, skills, strengths, and weaknesses. Tools: SWOT analysis of self, aptitude tests, personality tests (MBTI).
  2. Explore Career Opportunities
    Research available career paths, industries, job roles, and required qualifications.
  3. Set Career Goals
    Define short-term (1-2 years) and long-term (5-10 years) career objectives.
  4. Identify Development Needs
    Determine what skills, qualifications, and experiences are needed to achieve your goals.
  5. Prepare Action Plan
    Create a detailed plan with timelines, milestones, and specific development activities (certifications, training, networking).
  6. Review and Revise
    Regularly review progress and update the plan based on changing circumstances.

2. Career Development

Definition

Career Development is the ongoing process of acquiring skills, experiences, and education needed to progress along one's chosen career path. It combines both individual effort and organisational support.

Individual vs Organisational Responsibility

Individual ResponsibilitiesOrganisational Responsibilities
Self-assessment and goal settingProviding training and development programmes

NetworkingCareer counselling services
Continuous learning (certifications, degrees)Job rotation and stretch assignments

Career Development Tools

  • Job Ladders — defined vertical promotion paths within a function
  • Job Enrichment — adding more challenging responsibilities
  • Lateral Moves — moving to a different department at the same level to broaden experience
  • Dual Career Ladders — separate tracks for technical specialists and managers (both can advance without one forcing the other's path)
  • Mentoring Programmes — pairing junior employees with senior leaders
  • Assessment Centres — structured evaluation to identify high-potential employees

3. Succession Planning

Definition

Succession Planning is the systematic process of identifying, assessing, and developing internal talent to fill critical leadership and key positions in the future when they become vacant — due to retirement, resignation, or promotion.

Importance of Succession Planning

  • Ensures business continuity — no leadership vacuum
  • Reduces dependence on external hiring for senior positions
  • Motivates high-potential employees by showing a clear growth path
  • Reduces time and cost of finding replacements
  • Preserves institutional knowledge and organisational culture

Succession Planning Process

  1. Identify Key Positions
    Identify critical roles that, if left vacant, would significantly disrupt operations. (CEO, CFO, Plant Head, etc.)
  2. Identify High-Potential Employees
    Use performance appraisals, assessment centres, and manager nominations to identify HiPo talent.
  3. Assess Gaps
    Compare current capabilities of successors with what is required for the target position.
  4. Develop Successors
    Design Individual Development Plans (IDPs) — mentoring, stretch assignments, training, leadership programmes.
  5. Monitor and Review
    Regularly review the succession pipeline and update plans as business needs evolve.

Real-World Example

Tata Group: The appointment of N. Chandrasekaran as Chairman of Tata Sons was the result of a well-structured succession planning process. Similarly, Infosys has a formal leadership development programme (ILP+) that identifies and grooms future leaders from within.

Career Planning = individual-led goal setting and roadmap creation.

Career Development = acquiring skills; both individual and org responsibility.

Succession Planning = org-led process to prepare internal replacements for key roles.

Succession Planning ensures business continuity and motivates HiPo employees.

2-Mark Questions

2 marks Define Training. How is it different from Education? +

Training is a short-term, systematic process aimed at improving specific job-related knowledge, skills, and attitudes of employees to enhance their current job performance.

Difference: Training is job-oriented and focuses on present skills needed for a specific role. Education is broader, theoretical, and prepares a person for life and multiple career opportunities in general.

2 marks What is Training Need Analysis (TNA)? +

Training Need Analysis (TNA) is the process of identifying the gap between the current performance of employees and the desired/required performance standard. It determines what training is needed, for whom, and when.

TNA is conducted at three levels: Organisational Level, Task/Job Level, and Individual Level.

2 marks What is Vestibule Training? +

Vestibule Training is an off-the-job training method where employees are trained in a separate area (vestibule) using equipment and conditions that closely replicate the actual workplace — but without disrupting production or creating risk on the actual shop floor.

Example: Pilot training on flight simulators; bank teller training on dummy teller counters.

2 marks What is the Halo Effect in Performance Appraisal? +

Halo Effect is an appraisal error where the rater gives an overall high rating to an employee based on one outstanding positive characteristic, ignoring other aspects of performance.

Example: A manager rates a highly punctual employee as excellent in all areas — even though their work quality is only average.

2 marks Define Succession Planning. +

Succession Planning is the systematic process of identifying and developing internal talent (high-potential employees) to fill critical leadership and key positions when they become vacant in the future due to retirement, resignation, or promotion.

It ensures business continuity and creates a pipeline of ready leaders within the organisation.

2 marks What is Kirkpatrick's Level 1 and Level 2 of training evaluation? +

Level 1 — Reaction: Measures how trainees feel about the training programme — whether they found it useful, relevant, and well-delivered. Measured through feedback forms/smile sheets immediately after training.

Level 2 — Learning: Measures the actual knowledge, skills, or attitude gained from the training. Measured through pre-test vs. post-test comparisons or skill assessments.

5 marks Explain MBO as a technique of Performance Appraisal. +

Management by Objectives (MBO) was proposed by Peter Drucker and is one of the most popular modern methods of performance appraisal.

Meaning: MBO is a process where the manager and subordinate jointly set specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) objectives at the beginning of the appraisal period. At the end of the period, performance is evaluated based on whether those objectives were achieved.

Process of MBO:

  1. Manager and employee jointly set SMART objectives for the period.
  2. Employee works towards achieving the agreed objectives.
  3. Progress is reviewed periodically (monthly/quarterly).
  4. At the end of the period, actual achievement is compared against targets.
  5. New objectives are set for the next period based on learnings.

Advantages: Objective and transparent; increases employee motivation and ownership; aligns individual performance with organisational goals.

Disadvantages: Time-consuming to set objectives; difficult to apply for qualitative roles; may encourage short-term thinking.

Example: Google uses an advanced form of MBO called OKRs (Objectives and Key Results), where each employee sets quarterly objectives and tracks key results.

5 marks Explain 360-Degree Performance Appraisal with its advantages and limitations. +

360-Degree Appraisal is a modern, multi-source performance evaluation method where an employee is assessed by multiple stakeholders — including superiors, peers, subordinates, customers, and the employee themselves.

Sources of Feedback in 360°:

  • Superior/Boss — evaluates work output and targets met
  • Peers/Colleagues — evaluate teamwork and cooperation
  • Subordinates — evaluate leadership and managerial style
  • Customers (internal/external) — evaluate service quality
  • Self-appraisal — employee's own assessment

Advantages: Comprehensive and well-rounded view; reduces bias; improves self-awareness; useful for leadership development.

Limitations: Time-consuming and expensive; conflicting feedback can confuse employees; peers may give inflated ratings to friends; requires proper training to implement.

Example: Companies like Infosys, Wipro, and TCS use 360° appraisals for their managerial cadre.

5 marks Explain any four common errors in Performance Appraisal. +

Performance appraisal errors are mistakes made by raters that distort the accuracy of evaluation. Four important errors are:

1. Halo Effect: The rater gives an overall high rating based on one outstanding positive trait, ignoring other performance areas. Example: An employee who is always punctual gets rated "Excellent" in all parameters, even where performance is average.

2. Horn Effect: Opposite of halo — the rater gives overall low ratings due to one negative incident or trait. Example: An employee who missed one deadline is rated "Poor" across all dimensions.

3. Central Tendency: The rater rates everyone as average (3 out of 5) to avoid conflict or taking a definite position. This fails to distinguish between high and low performers.

4. Recency Error: The rater focuses only on recent performance (last few weeks) rather than the entire appraisal period. One recent incident — positive or negative — unduly influences the entire rating.

How to overcome: Train appraisers, use BARS or MBO, use 360° feedback, maintain critical incident diaries throughout the year.

10 marks Explain the Training Process in detail, with steps, and importance of Training Need Analysis. +

Introduction: Training is a systematic, short-term process of improving the knowledge, skills, and attitudes of employees to enable them to perform their current jobs effectively. A well-structured training process ensures that training is not random but strategic and result-oriented.

Steps in the Training Process:

Step 1 — Training Need Analysis (TNA): The foundation of any training programme. TNA identifies the gap between current performance and desired performance. It is conducted at three levels:
(a) Organisational level — based on company goals and strategy
(b) Task level — based on job requirements
(c) Individual level — based on personal performance gaps.

Step 2 — Setting Training Objectives: Based on the TNA, SMART objectives are defined — what employees should know, do, or feel differently after training.

Step 3 — Designing the Training Programme: Decisions are made on content, training methods (OJT or off-JT), trainers (internal/external), duration, venue, and budget.

Step 4 — Implementation: The training is delivered using selected methods — coaching, role play, simulation, e-learning, etc.

Step 5 — Evaluation: Effectiveness is measured using Kirkpatrick's four-level model: Reaction → Learning → Behaviour → Results.

Importance of TNA: TNA ensures that training resources are not wasted on irrelevant content. It aligns training with organisational strategy, identifies the right people to train, and serves as the benchmark against which training effectiveness is later evaluated.

Conclusion: A structured training process that begins with a thorough TNA and ends with rigorous evaluation ensures maximum return on training investment and contributes directly to organisational performance.

10 marks Explain Performance Appraisal: its process, methods, and common errors. (OR: Write a detailed note on Performance Appraisal.) +

Meaning: Performance Appraisal is the formal, systematic process of evaluating an employee's job performance against pre-established standards. It is one of the most critical functions of HRM, influencing salary, promotion, training, and motivation decisions.

Process of Performance Appraisal (6 Steps):

  1. Establish Standards — Set KPIs and targets aligned with org goals.
  2. Communicate Standards — Inform employees about evaluation criteria.
  3. Measure Actual Performance — Collect data through observation, records, feedback.
  4. Compare with Standards — Identify the performance gap.
  5. Discuss with Employee — Conduct appraisal interview; two-way communication.
  6. Corrective Action — Reward good performers; counsel/train poor performers.

Methods of Performance Appraisal:

Traditional: (1) Ranking Method — ranks employees from best to worst. (2) Graphic Rating Scale — numerical rating on multiple traits. (3) Checklist Method — tick-box of behavioural statements.

Modern: (1) 360-Degree Appraisal — multi-source feedback from superiors, peers, subordinates, customers, self. (2) MBO (Management by Objectives) — jointly set objectives; evaluate against targets. (3) BARS (Behaviorally Anchored Rating Scale) — scale anchored with specific behavioural examples.

Common Errors in Appraisal: Halo Effect (one positive dominates all ratings), Horn Effect (one negative dominates all ratings), Central Tendency (everyone rated average), Leniency Error (everyone rated high), Strictness Error (everyone rated low), Recency Error (only recent events considered), Similarity Bias (like-me bias).

Conclusion: Performance Appraisal, when done correctly and objectively, is a powerful tool for employee motivation, development, and organisational productivity. Using modern methods like MBO and 360°, combined with proper appraiser training, can significantly reduce errors and improve the accuracy of evaluations.

10 marks Describe various methods of training. Distinguish between On-the-Job and Off-the-Job training. +

Introduction: Training methods refer to the techniques used to impart knowledge, skills, and attitudes to employees. They are broadly classified into On-the-Job and Off-the-Job training methods.

On-the-Job Training (OJT) Methods: Employee learns by actually performing the job.

(1) Coaching — A senior employee (coach) provides one-on-one guidance and feedback on specific tasks. Personalised, continuous, and low cost. Example: Manager coaching a new sales rep.

(2) Mentoring — Long-term relationship where a senior mentor guides the mentee's overall career development. Focuses on career growth, unlike coaching which is task-focused.

(3) Job Rotation — Moving employees across different departments at intervals to build multi-skilling and reduce monotony. Example: Management trainees at Mahindra rotate across Finance, HR, Operations.

(4) Apprenticeship/Internship — Formal training contract (apprenticeship for skilled trades); temporary work experience (internship for fresh graduates).

Off-the-Job Training Methods: Employee learns away from the actual workplace.

(1) Vestibule Training — Training on replicated equipment in a separate area; no risk to production. Example: Flight simulators for pilots.

(2) Role Playing — Trainees act out real-life scenarios to develop interpersonal skills. Example: Customer complaint handling practice.

(3) Case Study Method — Real or hypothetical business problems are analysed to develop decision-making skills. Widely used in MBA programmes.

(4) Lecture Method — Traditional classroom delivery; cost-effective for large groups; passive learning.

(5) Simulation Training — Artificial environment replicating real conditions. Used in military, aviation, medical fields.

(6) E-Learning — Digital, self-paced learning through LMS, MOOCs, webinars. Scalable and flexible.

Key Differences — OJT vs Off-JT: OJT is at the actual workplace, low cost, learning by doing, employee produces output while learning. Off-JT is away from work, focused environment, no production, higher cost but more in-depth learning.

Conclusion: The choice of training method depends on the nature of the job, objectives of training, available resources, and number of trainees. A blended approach using both OJT and off-JT methods typically yields the best results.
