Labor Movement & Trade Unions

Definition: Labor Movement is the collective effort of workers to improve wages, conditions, and rights through organized action — trade unions, collective bargaining, and political lobbying.

Growth of Trade Unions in India

YearMilestone
1890Bombay Mill Hands Association — first union
1918Madras Labour Union — first registered union (B.P. Wadia)
1920AITUC — first national federation
1926Trade Unions Act — legal recognition given
1947+INTUC, HMS, BMS, CITU formed — political affiliations

Functions of Trade Unions

⚔ Militant

Collective bargaining, strikes, legal action

🤝 Fraternal

Welfare, financial aid, healthcare

🏛 Political

Lobbying, elections, legislation

💰 Economic

Wages, profit-sharing, bonus

Problems of Trade Unions

  • Multiplicity — too many small unions, weakens collective power
  • Political affiliation — political parties control unions for own agenda
  • Low membership — weak financial base
  • Outside leadership — politicians lead unions, not workers
  • Illiteracy — workers can't participate effectively
  • Employer hostility — management victimize union leaders
Mnemonic — MILL-PE: Multiplicity · Illiteracy · Low membership · Leadership (outside) · Political affiliation · Employer hostility
Exam tip: "Problems of trade unions" is asked in almost every exam. Write at least 5 problems with brief explanations.

Exam Q&A

2 marks What is a trade union?
A Trade Union is an organized association of workers formed to protect and promote their common interests in employment through collective action such as bargaining, strikes, and lobbying.
2 marks First trade union in India?
Madras Labour Union (1918) founded by B.P. Wadia is the first registered trade union. AITUC (1920) was the first national federation. Trade Unions Act 1926 gave legal recognition.
5 marks Explain functions of trade unions.
Trade unions perform 4 types of functions:

(1) Militant: Collective bargaining, strikes, gherao, legal action
(2) Fraternal: Welfare programs, financial aid, schools, hospitals for members
(3) Political: Lobbying, elections, representation in legislative bodies
(4) Economic: Wage improvement, profit sharing, bonus schemes

These functions collectively protect worker rights and improve employment conditions.

Collective Bargaining

ILO Definition: Collective Bargaining is the process of negotiation between employers and a group of employees aimed at agreements to regulate working salaries, conditions, benefits, and rights for workers.

Types of Collective Bargaining

TypeNatureExample
DistributiveWin-lose; fixed resourcesUnion wants 20% hike; company offers 10%
IntegrativeWin-win; mutual benefitFlexible hours in exchange for better benefits
ConcessionaryWorkers give up benefits to save jobsSalary cut to avoid layoffs
ProductivityWages linked to outputBonus for meeting production targets
CompositeWages + working conditions togetherWages + safety standards negotiated
Mnemonic — DICP-C: Distributive · Integrative · Concessionary · Productivity · Composite

Exam tip: 10-mark answer must include: Definition + All 5 Types + All 8 Process steps + Advantages/Disadvantages

Exam Q&A

2 marks Define Collective Bargaining.
Collective Bargaining is a process of negotiations between employer and workers' union representatives to determine wages, hours, benefits, and employment conditions, resulting in a binding collective agreement (ILO definition).
5 marks CB vs. Simple Negotiation — differences.
Collective Bargaining: Union vs. employer · legal framework (IDA 1947) · results in binding written contract · workers have collective power

Negotiation: Any two parties · generally informal · may/may not be written · individual power dynamics

Key difference: CB requires worker organization/union and has legal status; negotiation can be individual and informal.

Grievances & Grievance Handling

Definition (Michael Jucius): A grievance is "any dissatisfaction or feeling of injustice in connection with one's employment situation that is brought to the attention of management."

Grievance vs. Industrial Dispute

BasisGrievanceIndustrial Dispute
NatureIndividual complaintCollective formal conflict
PartiesSingle employee vs. mgmtUnion vs. management
Legal statusNo legal formalityGoverned by IDA 1947
ResolutionGrievance Handling ProcedureConciliation/Arbitration/Tribunal
ExampleUnhappy about transfer orderStrike over wage demands

Grievance Handling Procedure (GHP) — Flowchart

1
Employee → Supervisor — Express grievance verbally/written 3 days
↓ if unsatisfied
2
Supervisor's decision — Investigates and responds 2–3 days
↓ if unsatisfied
3
Department Head (HOD) — Reviews case, consults HR 3–5 days
↓ if unsatisfied
4
Grievance Committee — Joint management-worker committee meets 7 days
↓ if unsatisfied
5
Top Management / HR Director — Final internal appeal
↓ if unsatisfied
6
Voluntary Arbitration — Neutral third party decides
Good GHP features — SAFE-PW: Simple · Accurate · Fair · Effective · Prompt · Written records

Exam Q&A

2 marks Types of grievances.
Factual: Real concrete grievance with facts (e.g., denied promised promotion)
Imaginary: Based on wrong perception/misunderstanding
Disguised: Real cause hidden; another issue used as excuse
10 marks Explain GHP with flowchart.
Grievance Handling Procedure (GHP) is a systematic multi-step process. Steps: (1) Employee expresses to supervisor within 3 days; (2) Supervisor responds in 2-3 days; (3) If unsatisfied, escalates to HOD (3-5 days); (4) Grievance Committee of joint members meets within 7 days; (5) Appeal to Top Management/HR Director; (6) Voluntary Arbitration as final step; (7) If still unresolved → Labour Court under IDA 1947.

Features of good GHP: Simple, Fair, Prompt, Written records, Confidential, No victimization. Prevents individual grievances from becoming industrial disputes.

Employee Separation

Definition: Employee Separation is the process of terminating the employment relationship between an employee and organization, either voluntarily or involuntarily, temporarily or permanently.

Exam tip: This comparison table is asked in EVERY semester. Memorize: Layoff = temporary + 50% | Retrenchment = permanent + 15 days/year | Dismissal = misconduct + no compensation.

Exam Q&A

2 marks Difference: layoff vs. retrenchment.
Layoff: Temporary removal due to external reasons (shortage, recession); 50% basic wages as compensation; worker may return.

Retrenchment: Permanent termination for economic reasons (downsizing, automation); 15 days wages per year of service; LIFO principle; Govt permission for 100+ workers; worker does not return.
5 marks Explain types of employee separation.
1. Resignation: Voluntary exit by employee; notice period served; full & final settlement.
2. Retirement: At prescribed age (superannuation, 58-60 yrs) or VRS; gratuity + PF + pension.
3. Layoff: Temporary, employer-initiated, external reasons; 50% wage compensation; IDA 1947.
4. Retrenchment: Permanent, economic reasons; 15 days/year compensation; LIFO; Govt permission (100+).
5. Termination: Involuntary; poor performance or business closure.
6. Dismissal: For misconduct; domestic enquiry mandatory; no compensation.

Employee Safety & Health

Definition: Occupational Safety and Health (OSH) is concerned with safety, health, and welfare of people at work — identifying, evaluating, and controlling workplace hazards to foster a safe work environment.

5 Types of Workplace Hazards

Physical

Noise, vibration, extreme heat/cold, radiation → Hearing loss, burns

Chemical

Toxic gases, solvents, acids → Poisoning, cancer, respiratory

Biological

Bacteria, viruses, fungi → Infections, hepatitis, TB

Ergonomic

Repetitive motion, poor posture → CTS, back pain

Psychological

Stress, harassment, bullying → Burnout, anxiety, depression → Remedied by EAP, POSH Act

Mnemonic — PCBEP: Physical · Chemical · Biological · Ergonomic · Psychological

Controls Hierarchy (Most to Least Preferred)

1
Engineering Controls — Machine guarding, ventilation, ergonomic design (most effective)
↓
2
Administrative Controls — SOPs, job rotation, time limits on hazardous areas
↓
3
PPE — Helmets, masks, gloves, goggles (last resort, least effective alone)

Key Occupational Diseases

  • Silicosis — silica dust → mining/construction
  • Asbestosis — asbestos fibers → shipbuilding
  • Byssinosis — cotton dust → textile (Brown Lung)
  • CTS (Carpal Tunnel Syndrome) — repetitive motion → IT/data entry
  • Noise-Induced Hearing Loss — prolonged loud noise → manufacturing

Industrial Disputes Act, 1947

Section 2(k): Industrial Dispute = "Any dispute or difference between employers and employers, or between employers and workmen, or between workmen and workmen, connected with employment or non-employment or terms of employment or conditions of labour."

Factories Act, 1948

Factory (S.2m): Any premises where 10 or more workers work with the aid of power, or 20 or more workers work without power, in a manufacturing process.

Key Provisions by Chapter

Chapter III — Health

S.11 Clean · S.13 Ventilation · S.17 Lighting · S.18 Drinking water · S.19 Latrines

Chapter IV — Safety

S.21 Machine fencing · S.22 No cleaning near moving parts · S.38 Fire safety · S.40 Safety Officer (1000+)

Chapter V — Welfare

S.45 First aid · S.46 Canteen · S.47 Shelter · S.48 Creche · S.49 Welfare Officer

Mnemonic — 250-150-30-500-1000: Canteen(250) · Shelter(150) · Creche(30 women) · Welfare Officer(500) · Safety Officer(1000)

Working Hours

  • Daily: maximum 9 hours
  • Weekly: maximum 48 hours
  • Overtime wages: double the ordinary rate
  • Spread over: max 10.5 hours
  • Rest interval: 30 min after 5 continuous hours
  • No child under 14 years (S.67)
  • Women: cannot work between 7 PM – 6 AM
Penalties: Contravention → 2 years + Rs.1 lakh fine | Death/serious injury → 3 years + Rs.3 lakh | Continuing offence → Rs.1,000/day extra

POSH Act, 2013

Based on: Vishakha & Others v. State of Rajasthan (1997) — Supreme Court guidelines on sexual harassment at workplace, which became the foundation for POSH Act 2013.
Sexual Harassment (S.2n): Includes — (i) physical contact/advances, (ii) demand/request for sexual favors, (iii) sexually colored remarks, (iv) showing pornography, (v) any unwelcome physical/verbal/non-verbal conduct of sexual nature.

Two Types of Sexual Harassment

Quid Pro Quo

"This for that" — sexual favors demanded for job benefits (promotion, hiring)

Hostile Work Environment

Conduct so pervasive it creates intimidating/offensive atmosphere

ICC — Internal Complaints Committee (S.4)

Mandatory for every employer with 10+ employees. Must be formed in EACH office/branch.
MemberRequirement
Presiding OfficerSenior woman employee at the workplace
Internal Members (2)Employees committed to women's cause / legal knowledge
External Member (1)NGO/association working for women OR expert on SH issues
Gender ruleAt least 50% members must be women
TenureMaximum 3 years

Complaint Procedure Flowchart

1
Written complaint to ICC/LCC — within 3 months of incident (extendable to 6 months)
↓
2
Conciliation (optional) — if complainant requests; no monetary settlement allowed
↓
3
Inquiry — ICC conducts inquiry; both parties heard; principles of natural justice 90 days
↓
4
ICC submits report to employer within 10 days of inquiry completion
↓
5
Employer takes action on ICC recommendations 60 days
↓
6
Appeal to court/tribunal within 90 days
POSH Timelines — 3-90-60-90: 3 months to file · 90 days inquiry · 60 days employer action · 90 days to appeal
Employer penalty for NOT forming ICC: Rs. 50,000 fine + possible license cancellation on repeat offence

Employer's Responsibilities

  • Constitute ICC in every office with 10+ employees
  • Display consequences of SH at conspicuous places
  • Conduct regular awareness and sensitization programs
  • Ensure confidentiality of complaints and proceedings
  • Treat SH as misconduct in service rules
  • Submit annual report to District Officer
