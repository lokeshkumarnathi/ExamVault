1 — Compensation: Foundations & Key Concepts

University Exam Definition — Compensation

Compensation is the total package of monetary and non-monetary rewards provided by an organisation to employees in exchange for their work, time, skills, and commitment. It includes wages, salaries, incentives, benefits, and all other forms of remuneration.

Types of Compensation

Direct Compensation

Financial payments made directly to employees for work performed.

  • Basic wage / salary
  • Dearness Allowance (DA)
  • House Rent Allowance (HRA)
  • Overtime pay
  • Bonus and incentives
  • Commission

Indirect Compensation (Benefits)

Non-cash rewards and services provided to employees.

  • Health and accident insurance
  • Provident Fund (PF)
  • Gratuity
  • Paid leave (casual, sick, earned)
  • Canteen, transport, housing
  • Recreational facilities

Basic Factors in Determining Pay Rates

Pay rates are influenced by multiple internal and external factors:

Internal Factors

  • Job evaluation — relative worth of job
  • Employee performance — merit-based pay
  • Seniority / experience — years of service
  • Skill and qualifications — educational level
  • Organisation's ability to pay — profitability
  • Company pay policy — lead, lag, or match market
  • Trade union bargaining power

External Factors

  • Labour market conditions — demand vs supply
  • Cost of living / inflation — CPI index
  • Government legislation — Minimum Wages Act
  • Industry pay standards — market benchmarks
  • Competitor pay levels — market surveys
  • Economic conditions — recession, growth
  • Collective bargaining — union agreements

Quick Revision — 1

Compensation = all monetary + non-monetary rewards. Broadest term.

Wage = hourly/daily (blue-collar). Salary = monthly fixed (white-collar).

Direct = cash payments. Indirect = benefits (PF, insurance, canteen).

Internal equity = fairness within org (via job evaluation). External equity = fairness vs market (via pay surveys).

Factors: internal (job, skills, performance, policy) + external (market, govt law, CPI, unions).

2 — Job Evaluation Methods

Definition — Job Evaluation

Job Evaluation is a systematic process of determining the relative worth or value of jobs within an organisation in order to establish a rational, equitable, and consistent pay structure. It compares jobs, not people.

Objectives of Job Evaluation

  • Establish a fair and rational wage structure
  • Eliminate pay inequalities and discrimination
  • Provide a basis for wage negotiations with unions
  • Help in proper classification of jobs
  • Facilitate recruitment, promotion, and transfer decisions

Method 1 — Ranking Method

Definition

The Ranking Method is the simplest job evaluation method where jobs are arranged in order from highest to lowest based on overall job worth or importance, without analysing individual job factors.

Process

  1. Prepare job descriptions for all jobs
  2. Evaluators study each job holistically
  3. Jobs are ranked from most important to least important
  4. Pay grades assigned based on rank

Advantages

  • Simple and quick
  • Low cost
  • Easy to explain to employees

Disadvantages

  • Highly subjective — no scientific basis
  • Does not show how much one job is worth vs another
  • Not suitable for large organisations

Example: CEO > General Manager > Department Manager > Supervisor > Clerk — jobs simply ranked in order.

Method 2 — Classification / Grading Method

Definition

The Classification (Grading) Method pre-defines a set of job grades/classes with descriptions, and then each job is placed into the most appropriate grade based on how well it matches the grade description.

Process

  1. Define pay grades (e.g., Grade I to Grade V)
  2. Write a description for each grade (responsibilities, skills needed)
  3. Evaluate each job and slot it into the matching grade
  4. Assign pay scales to each grade

Advantages

  • More systematic than ranking
  • Easy to administer
  • Used widely in government/PSUs

Disadvantages

  • Grade descriptions can be vague
  • Fitting jobs into grades is subjective
  • Not suitable for highly diverse jobs

Example: Indian government Pay Bands — Grade Pay ₹1,800 (peon) to ₹10,000 (IAS officer). All jobs are slotted into predefined pay bands.

Method 3 — Point Rating Method

Definition

The Point Rating Method is the most widely used and scientific job evaluation method. Each job is broken down into specific factors (skill, effort, responsibility, working conditions), each factor is assigned maximum points, and jobs are scored on each factor. Total points determine job worth and pay grade.

Process

  1. Select compensable factors (e.g., education, experience, effort, responsibility, conditions)
  2. Assign maximum points to each factor based on importance
  3. Define degrees/levels within each factor (e.g., Low/Medium/High)
  4. Score each job on every factor
  5. Add up total points per job
  6. Convert points to pay grades

Example Factor Table:

Advantages

  • Most objective and scientific
  • Reduces bias
  • Easy to explain to employees
  • Can be applied to diverse jobs

Disadvantages

  • Time-consuming to develop
  • Factor selection can be subjective
  • Expensive to maintain

Method 4 — Factor Comparison Method

Definition

The Factor Comparison Method is a quantitative technique where a set of key benchmark jobs are evaluated factor by factor, and their current pay is distributed among the factors. All other jobs are then compared against these benchmark jobs factor by factor to determine their pay.

Process

  1. Select 15–20 key benchmark jobs (jobs whose pay is already accepted as fair)
  2. Identify compensable factors (typically: mental requirements, skill, physical effort, responsibility, working conditions)
  3. Rank each key job on each factor
  4. Allocate current pay to each factor for each key job
  5. Compare all other jobs against the key jobs factor by factor
  6. Add up factor pay values to get total pay for each job

Advantages

  • Scientific and systematic
  • Provides actual monetary values
  • Useful for diverse job families

Disadvantages

  • Complex and difficult to understand
  • Very time-consuming
  • If key jobs are wrongly selected, entire system fails

3 — Establishing Pay Rates & Pricing Managerial Jobs

Establishing Pay Rates — Process

  1. Conduct Job Analysis
    Analyse each job to understand its duties, responsibilities, requirements, and working conditions. Output: Job Description + Job Specification.
  2. Perform Job Evaluation
    Use one of the four methods (Ranking, Classification, Point Rating, Factor Comparison) to determine the relative worth of each job.
  3. Conduct Pay / Wage Survey
    Collect data on pay rates offered by competitor organisations and the industry for similar jobs. Sources: NASSCOM surveys, Mercer surveys, Aon Hewitt reports.
  4. Determine Pay Policy
    Decide whether the organisation will lead (pay above market), match (pay at market), or lag (pay below market). Also decide pay mix — how much is fixed vs variable.
  5. Group Jobs into Pay Grades
    Cluster jobs of similar worth into pay grades (bands). Each grade has a minimum, midpoint, and maximum pay rate.
  6. Develop Pay Ranges (Wage Structure)
    Create a wage curve or salary ranges for each pay grade. Allow for salary progression within grades based on performance or seniority.
  7. Fine-Tune and Implement
    Handle red-circle (overpaid) and green-circle (underpaid) employees. Communicate the pay structure and implement. Review periodically.

Market-Based Pay Systems

In a market-based pay system, salaries are set primarily by what the external labour market pays for similar jobs. The organisation regularly surveys the market and benchmarks its pay against the 25th, 50th (median), or 75th percentile of market pay data.

StrategyApproachWhen Used
Lead the MarketPay above 75th percentileAttract top talent; tech companies like Google, Infosys
Match the MarketPay at 50th percentile (median)Most organisations; standard approach
Lag the MarketPay below 50th percentileCost-saving; offset with other benefits

Pricing Managerial and Professional Jobs

Concept

Pricing managerial and professional jobs is more complex than pricing operative jobs because managerial work is less standardised, involves higher discretion, and pay must reflect the strategic value and market demand for talent. Traditional job evaluation methods are often inadequate for such roles.

Special Features of Managerial Pay

  • Base salary — fixed monthly pay reflecting job level and market value
  • Short-term incentives — annual bonus linked to company or division performance (typically 10–50% of base)
  • Long-term incentives — stock options, ESOPs (Employee Stock Ownership Plans), RSUs (Restricted Stock Units) to align manager interest with shareholders
  • Perquisites (Perks) — company car, club membership, driver, housing allowance, business class travel
  • Benefits — enhanced health cover, superannuation pension, key-man insurance

Factors Specific to Managerial Pay

  • Level in organisational hierarchy (CEO vs VP vs Manager)
  • Business complexity and size (revenue, team size)
  • Industry and market demand for that skill set
  • Company performance and profitability
  • Individual's track record, qualifications, and negotiating power

Real-World Examples

4 — Performance-Based Pay

Definition — Performance-Based Pay

Performance-Based Pay (PBP) is a compensation system where an employee's pay is linked directly to their individual, team, or organisational performance. Unlike fixed pay, PBP varies based on achievement of goals and targets. It is designed to motivate employees and align their efforts with organisational objectives.

Objectives of Performance-Based Pay

  • Motivate employees to achieve higher performance
  • Align individual goals with organisational objectives
  • Reward high performers differentially
  • Reduce fixed labour costs by making part of pay variable
  • Attract and retain high-performing talent

1. Incentives

Definition

Incentives are additional payments made to employees over and above their regular wages/salary to motivate them to achieve higher levels of performance.

Individual Incentives

  • Piece Rate — paid per unit produced (e.g., garment worker paid ₹20 per shirt stitched)
  • Standard Hour Plan — bonus for completing work faster than standard time
  • Merit Pay Increase — permanent salary increase based on performance rating

Group/Org Incentives

  • Team incentive — bonus shared among team for reaching group targets
  • Gain Sharing — sharing cost savings or productivity gains with employees
  • Profit Sharing — distributing a percentage of company profits

2. Bonus

Definition

A bonus is a lump-sum payment made to employees in addition to their regular pay, typically as a reward for achieving specific performance targets, or as a statutory requirement (festival bonus under the Payment of Bonus Act, 1965).

  • Statutory / Festival Bonus — mandated by Payment of Bonus Act; minimum 8.33% of annual salary (up to ₹21,000/month basic salary eligible)
  • Performance Bonus — tied to individual/company performance; paid annually
  • Sign-on Bonus — paid when joining a new company to cover leaving-bonus loss
  • Retention Bonus — paid to retain a key employee for a specific period

Example: TCS pays an annual performance bonus of 10–20% of CTC to employees who achieve "A" or "B+" ratings. Infosys paid a ₹8,000 special bonus to all employees during post-COVID recovery.

3. Commission

Definition

Commission is a performance-based pay where employees (typically in sales roles) earn a percentage of the sales revenue or profit they generate.

TypeDescriptionExample
Straight Commission100% of pay is commission; no base salaryInsurance agents (₹0 base + 15% of premium)
Salary + CommissionFixed base salary plus commission on salesHUL sales rep: ₹25,000 base + 2% of sales
Draw Against CommissionAdvance drawn from future commission earningsReal estate agents

4. Profit Sharing

Definition

Profit Sharing is a plan under which employees receive a share of the organisation's annual profits, typically as a lump-sum payment or contribution to a retirement fund. It links employee welfare to company success.

  • Cash Plan — a portion of profits paid directly as cash bonus
  • Deferred Plan — profits credited to an employee's retirement account (e.g., superannuation fund)
  • Combination Plan — part cash + part deferred

Example: Tata Group companies have historically shared profits with employees. Infosys ESOP (Employee Stock Option Plan) is a form of long-term profit sharing where employees gain from share price appreciation.

5. Merit Pay

Definition

Merit Pay is a permanent increase in an employee's base salary based on their annual performance appraisal rating. Unlike a bonus (which is a one-time payment), merit pay becomes part of the employee's ongoing salary.

5 — Employee Benefits & Welfare Facilities

Definition — Employee Benefits

Employee Benefits are non-wage compensation elements provided to employees in addition to their regular salary. They are indirect forms of financial compensation designed to improve employee well-being, security, and quality of life.

Insurance Benefits

Health Insurance

Covers hospitalisation, surgical, and medical expenses of employees and often their family members.

  • Group mediclaim policy: most Indian companies provide ₹3–10 lakh annual cover
  • Covers in-patient (IPD) and sometimes out-patient (OPD) expenses
  • Pre-existing conditions may be covered after a waiting period
  • Example: Wipro provides employees with ₹5 lakh group health insurance under Star Health

Accident Insurance

Provides compensation to employees or their nominees in case of death or disability due to workplace accidents.

  • Group Personal Accident (GPA) policy
  • Covers: accidental death, permanent total/partial disability, temporary disability
  • Governed by: Employees' Compensation Act (formerly Workmen's Compensation Act)
  • Example: Construction companies mandatorily insure workers for accidental death

Medical Benefits

  • ESI (Employees' State Insurance) — statutory scheme covering employees earning up to ₹21,000/month; provides free medical care, sick pay, maternity benefit
  • Company dispensary — on-site medical facility with doctor and nurse
  • Reimbursement — employees submit medical bills for reimbursement (annual limit)
  • Free diagnostic tests — some MNCs offer annual health checkup

Retirement Benefits

Provident Fund (PF)

A statutory retirement savings scheme under the Employees' Provident Fund Act, 1952.

  • Employee contributes 12% of basic salary
  • Employer also contributes 12% (8.33% to EPS pension, 3.67% to EPF)
  • Earns interest (~8.15% p.a.)
  • Withdrawable at retirement, resignation, or partial withdrawal for emergency

Gratuity

A lump-sum payment made by employer to an employee on separation after completing 5 years of service, under the Payment of Gratuity Act, 1972.

  • Formula: (Last basic salary × 15 × years of service) ÷ 26
  • Maximum gratuity: ₹20 lakh (tax-exempt)
  • Payable on: retirement, resignation (min. 5 yrs), death, disability
  • Example: 20 yrs service, basic ₹30,000/month → Gratuity = (30,000 × 15 × 20)/26 = ₹3,46,153

Pension

A regular monthly income provided to employees after retirement.

  • EPS (Employee Pension Scheme) — under EPF; employer contributes 8.33% to EPS; eligible for pension after 10 years service + age 58
  • NPS (National Pension System) — market-linked pension scheme; central/state govt employees mandatorily enrolled; corporates may offer voluntarily
  • Superannuation Fund — some companies (like LIC, banks) maintain additional pension funds for employees

Employee Welfare Facilities

Definition — Employee Welfare

Employee Welfare refers to all activities, facilities, and services provided by an employer (and sometimes the government) to improve the working and living conditions of employees and their families, beyond their regular wages. It covers both workplace welfare and community welfare.

Types of Welfare Facilities

Intra-Mural (Within the Workplace)

  • Canteen — subsidised or free meals facility at the workplace. Mandatory under Factories Act for establishments with 250+ workers
  • Drinking water — clean potable water facility
  • Rest rooms and crèche — rest areas and childcare facility (mandatory for 30+ women workers)
  • Washing and toilet facilities — clean sanitation
  • First aid boxes — one per 150 workers
  • Safety measures — protective equipment, fire extinguishers, safety training
  • Ambulance facility — for factories with 500+ workers

Extra-Mural (Outside the Workplace)

  • Housing — company-provided or subsidised accommodation; especially in industrial townships (e.g., BHEL Bhopal, Jamshedpur Tata Steel)
  • Transport — free or subsidised bus service for employees to/from office
  • Educational facilities — schools/colleges for employees' children; scholarships
  • Medical facilities — company hospitals or dispensaries for employees and family
  • Recreational facilities — sports clubs, gymnasiums, cultural activities
  • Consumer cooperative stores — subsidised grocery and goods for employees
  • Holiday homes / rest houses — subsidised vacation facilities

Safety Measures in Detail

  • Provision of Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) — helmets, gloves, safety shoes, goggles
  • Regular safety training and fire drills
  • Machinery guards and safety valves on pressure vessels
  • Proper lighting and ventilation
  • Hazardous materials handling procedures
  • Emergency evacuation plans and fire exits
  • Accident reporting and investigation systems
  • Compliance with Factories Act 1948 safety provisions

Importance of Employee Welfare

  • Improves employee morale, motivation, and loyalty
  • Reduces absenteeism and employee turnover
  • Improves productivity and work quality
  • Builds a positive employer brand for recruitment
  • Reduces industrial disputes and strikes
  • Fulfils social and legal obligations of the employer
  • Improves physical and mental health of workforce

Quick Revision — Unit 5

PF: Employee 12% + Employer 12% of basic. Governed by EPF Act, 1952.

Gratuity: (Last basic × 15 × years) ÷ 26. Needs min. 5 years service. Max ₹20 lakh.

ESI: covers employees earning up to ₹21,000/month. Provides free medical, sick pay, maternity benefit.

Canteen: mandatory for 250+ workers. Crèche: mandatory for 30+ women workers. First aid: 1 per 150 workers.

Intra-mural = within workplace. Extra-mural = outside workplace (housing, transport, schools).

6 — Labour Laws: Workmen's Compensation Act & Minimum Wages Act

Workmen's Compensation Act, 1923

Note: Renamed "Employees' Compensation Act" in 2009; exam questions still commonly use the original name.

Meaning and Objective

Definition

The Workmen's Compensation Act, 1923 is a social security legislation that makes it mandatory for employers to pay compensation to workers or their dependents in case of accidental injuries, occupational diseases, or death arising out of and in the course of employment.

Objectives

  • Provide financial protection to workers injured at work
  • Compensate dependents in case of worker's death due to work accident
  • Place the burden of industrial injuries on the employer (employer's liability)
  • Eliminate need for workers to file civil suits to get compensation

Scope and Coverage

  • Applies to workers in: factories, mines, plantations, construction sites, transport establishments, railways, ships, and hazardous occupations listed in Schedule II of the Act
  • Applies to workers earning up to ₹15,000/month (before 2009 amendment) — the wage ceiling has been revised over time
  • Does NOT apply to members of Armed Forces or workers covered under ESI Act (those are covered by ESI separately)

Employer's Duties / Responsibilities

  • Pay compensation within one month of accident/death
  • Notify the Commissioner if a worker dies or is seriously injured at work
  • Maintain a register of accidents
  • Cannot contract out of liability (cannot make worker waive right to compensation)

Commissioner

  • A Commissioner for Workmen's Compensation is appointed (typically a Labour Officer)
  • Settles disputes over compensation amount or liability
  • Can award interest if employer delays compensation payment
  • Has powers of a civil court
Mnemonic — When Compensation is NOT Payable (3 exceptions)

"ADD" — Alcohol/drugs · Disobedience of safety orders · Deliberate removal of safety guard

Minimum Wages Act, 1948

Meaning and Objective

Definition

The Minimum Wages Act, 1948 is a legislation that empowers the Central and State Governments to fix and revise minimum wages for workers in scheduled (notified) employments to protect workers from exploitation and ensure a minimum standard of living.

Objectives

  • Prevent exploitation of workers by paying unjustly low wages
  • Ensure workers receive wages sufficient to meet basic needs (food, clothing, shelter)
  • Reduce wage inequality across regions and industries
  • Provide a legal minimum floor below which no employer can pay

Scheduled Employments (Applies To)

The Act applies to scheduled employments listed in the Act's schedule, including:

  • Agriculture / farms
  • Construction work
  • Mining
  • Bidi making
  • Stone breaking and crushing
  • Road and water construction
  • Domestic work
  • Any employment notified by State or Central Government

Salient Features of the Minimum Wages Act, 1948

  1. Fixing Minimum Wages: Both Central and State governments can fix minimum wages for scheduled employments. Central government fixes wages for central sphere (railways, mines, ports). State governments fix for state sphere industries.
  2. Components of Minimum Wage: Includes basic rate + special allowance (dearness allowance to offset inflation). Some definitions also include the value of house accommodation or other amenities.
  3. Revision: Minimum wages must be reviewed and revised at least every 5 years. Dearness allowance may be revised more frequently based on the Consumer Price Index (CPI).
  4. Types of Minimum Wage:
    — Statutory Minimum Wage — legally fixed floor
    — Need-Based Minimum Wage — based on minimum standard of living
    — Fair Wage — above minimum, based on capacity of industry to pay
  5. Employer's Obligation: No employer can pay below the minimum wage. An employer who pays below minimum wage is liable to pay the deficit amount plus equal penalty and can be prosecuted.
  6. Working Hours: The Act also empowers governments to fix hours of work for scheduled employment. Standard working day = 9 hours (including rest intervals). Maximum work week = 48 hours.
  7. Overtime: Any work beyond 9 hours per day or 48 hours per week must be paid at double the normal rate (overtime rate = 2× minimum wage rate).
  8. Inspectors: Government appoints Inspectors to check whether minimum wages are being paid. They can enter premises, examine records, and take samples.
  9. Penalties for Violation: Imprisonment up to 6 months, or fine up to ₹500, or both (for each offence). For repeat offence, higher penalties apply.
  10. Claim for Unpaid Wages: Workers can file a claim before the Authority within 6 months of the breach to recover unpaid difference plus compensation (up to 10× the shortfall amount).

2-Mark Questions

2 marksDefine Compensation. Distinguish between Wage and Salary.+

Compensation is the total package of monetary and non-monetary rewards — including wages, salaries, incentives, benefits, and perks — provided by an organisation to employees in exchange for their services.

Wage vs Salary: A wage is payment calculated on an hourly or daily basis, typically for manual/blue-collar workers (e.g., factory worker paid ₹500/day). A salary is a fixed, regular monthly payment for white-collar/office workers (e.g., HR manager paid ₹40,000/month). Wages are variable with hours worked; salary is fixed regardless of hours.

2 marksWhat is Job Evaluation? What is its purpose?+

Job Evaluation is a systematic process of determining the relative worth or value of different jobs within an organisation, in order to establish an equitable and consistent pay structure. It compares jobs — not the people performing those jobs.

Purpose: To eliminate pay inequalities, provide a rational basis for fixing wages, facilitate wage negotiations, and ensure internal equity (fair pay differentials between jobs).

2 marksWhat is the Minimum Wages Act, 1948? What is its objective?+

The Minimum Wages Act, 1948 is a legislation that empowers Central and State Governments to fix and revise minimum wages for workers in scheduled employments, to prevent their exploitation and ensure they receive sufficient wages to meet basic needs.

Objective: To fix a legal floor below which no employer can pay; to protect workers from underpayment; to ensure a minimum standard of living for workers in scheduled industries.

2 marksDefine Gratuity. What is the formula for calculating it?+

Gratuity is a lump-sum retirement benefit paid by an employer to an employee under the Payment of Gratuity Act, 1972, upon completion of at least 5 years of continuous service, as a reward for long and meritorious service.

Formula: Gratuity = (Last drawn basic salary × 15 × years of service) ÷ 26

Maximum gratuity payable is ₹20 lakh (tax-exempt). Example: Basic salary ₹30,000/month, 20 years service → Gratuity = (30,000 × 15 × 20) ÷ 26 = ₹3,46,153.

5-Mark Questions

5 marksExplain the Point Rating Method of Job Evaluation.+

Introduction: The Point Rating Method is the most widely used quantitative method of job evaluation. It analyses jobs by breaking them into specific compensable factors, assigning maximum points to each factor, and scoring each job accordingly.

Process:

  1. Select Compensable Factors: Identify factors relevant to all jobs being evaluated — typically: education, experience, mental effort, physical effort, responsibility, and working conditions.
  2. Assign Maximum Points: Allocate maximum points to each factor based on its relative importance. Example: Responsibility = 150 pts (most important), Working Conditions = 50 pts.
  3. Define Degrees: Each factor is divided into levels (degrees) — e.g., Low, Medium, High — with specific point values for each degree.
  4. Score Each Job: For every job, rate it on each factor based on job description and specification.
  5. Total Points: Add up all factor points for each job. The total determines the job's worth.
  6. Convert to Pay Grades: Jobs with similar total points are grouped into the same pay grade with a corresponding salary range.

Advantages: Most objective and scientific; reduces personal bias; can be applied to diverse jobs; easy to explain to employees.

Disadvantages: Time-consuming and expensive to develop; selection of factors and weights can itself be subjective; requires regular updating.

Example: Hay Group's widely-used job evaluation system is based on point factor methodology, used by companies like Tata Steel and Unilever.

5 marksExplain the salient features of the Workmen's Compensation Act, 1923.+

Introduction: The Workmen's Compensation Act, 1923 (renamed Employees' Compensation Act, 2009) is a landmark social security legislation that provides financial protection to workers who are injured or killed in the course of employment.

Salient Features:

  1. Employer's Liability: The employer is liable to pay compensation when a worker suffers accidental injury or death arising out of and in the course of employment. The burden is on the employer, not the worker.
  2. Coverage: Applies to workers in factories, mines, construction sites, transport, railways, ships, and all scheduled hazardous occupations (Schedule II).
  3. Types of Disablement Covered: (a) Death (b) Permanent Total Disablement (c) Permanent Partial Disablement (d) Temporary Disablement (minimum 3 days).
  4. Compensation Amounts: Death = 50% of wages × relevant factor (min ₹1,20,000). Permanent Total Disablement = 60% of wages × relevant factor (min ₹1,40,000). Temporary disablement = 25% of wages for up to 5 years.
  5. Occupational Diseases: Schedule III lists occupational diseases (e.g., silicosis, anthrax, lead poisoning). Contracting a scheduled disease is treated as an employment injury.
  6. Exceptions: No compensation if injury is due to worker's intoxication, wilful disobedience of safety rules, or deliberate removal of safety devices.
  7. Commissioner: A Commissioner for Workmen's Compensation is appointed to adjudicate disputes. The Commissioner has powers of a civil court.
  8. Payment within one month: Compensation must be paid within one month of the accident. If delayed, interest is charged on the unpaid amount.

Conclusion: This Act protects workers from the financial devastation of work-related injuries and promotes employer responsibility for workplace safety.

10-Mark Essay Questions

10 marksExplain all methods of Job Evaluation in detail. Which method is the best and why?+

Introduction: Job Evaluation is a systematic process of determining the relative worth of jobs within an organisation to establish an equitable pay structure. There are four major methods of job evaluation.

1. Ranking Method: The simplest method where jobs are ranked from highest to lowest based on overall importance without analysing individual factors. Process: prepare job descriptions → rank jobs holistically → assign pay based on rank. Advantages: simple, quick, low cost. Disadvantages: highly subjective, not suitable for large organisations, no quantitative basis. Best for: small organisations.

2. Classification/Grading Method: Predefined job grades are established with descriptions, and each job is placed into the appropriate grade. Widely used in government organisations. Process: define grades → write grade descriptions → slot jobs into grades → assign pay scales. Advantages: systematic, easy to administer. Disadvantages: grade descriptions may be vague, subjective fitting. Example: Indian government Pay Bands.

3. Point Rating Method: The most popular quantitative method. Jobs are broken into compensable factors (education, experience, responsibility, effort, conditions), each factor is assigned maximum points, and jobs are scored on each factor. Total points determine pay grade. Advantages: most objective, reduces bias, applicable to diverse jobs. Disadvantages: expensive to develop, time-consuming. Example: Hay Group methodology used by Tata Steel, Unilever.

4. Factor Comparison Method: Uses benchmark jobs as reference points. The pay of benchmark jobs is distributed among compensable factors. Other jobs are then compared to benchmark jobs factor by factor to determine their pay. Advantages: scientific, gives monetary values. Disadvantages: very complex, if benchmark jobs are incorrectly chosen the whole system fails.

Best Method: The Point Rating Method is considered the best because it is the most objective and scientific; it provides a quantitative score for every job; it reduces personal bias; it can be explained clearly to employees and unions; and it is flexible enough to be applied across diverse job families. It is the most widely used method in Indian and global organisations.

Conclusion: While the choice of method depends on organisational size and resources, the Point Rating Method provides the best balance of objectivity, fairness, and practicability for most organisations.

10 marksExplain Performance-Based Pay in detail. What are its various components and importance?+

Meaning: Performance-Based Pay (PBP) is a compensation philosophy where part or all of an employee's pay varies with their individual, team, or organisational performance. It replaces or supplements fixed pay with variable pay tied to achieving predetermined targets.

Importance of PBP: (1) Motivates employees to achieve higher performance. (2) Aligns employee goals with organisational objectives. (3) Differentiates rewards between high and low performers. (4) Controls fixed labour costs by making pay variable. (5) Attracts and retains high-performing talent.

Components of Performance-Based Pay:

1. Incentives: Additional payments over regular wage for exceeding output targets. Individual incentives include piece rate (paid per unit produced), standard hour plans, and merit increases. Group incentives include gain sharing (sharing cost savings) and team bonuses. Example: A garment factory worker paid ₹20 per shirt stitched beyond 100 units/day.

2. Bonus: A lump-sum payment for achieving specific targets or as a statutory requirement. Types: performance bonus (10–20% of salary for high raters), sign-on bonus (when joining), retention bonus, festival/statutory bonus (minimum 8.33% under Payment of Bonus Act). Example: TCS annual performance bonus of 15% for "A" rated employees.

3. Commission: A percentage of sales revenue paid to sales employees. Types: straight commission (only commission, no base), salary plus commission (mixed), draw against commission. Example: Insurance agents earning 15% of premium collected. HUL sales reps earning ₹25,000 base + 2% of monthly sales.

4. Profit Sharing: Distributing a portion of annual company profits to employees. Builds a sense of ownership. Types: cash plan (direct cash), deferred plan (into pension/ESOP), combination. Example: Infosys ESOP enables employees to benefit from company share price growth.

5. Merit Pay: A permanent increase in base salary based on annual performance appraisal rating — unlike bonus which is a one-time payment. Outstanding performers receive 8–12% merit increase; average performers receive 2–4%. This rewards consistent good performers cumulatively over their career.

Conclusion: Performance-Based Pay is a powerful strategic tool when designed and communicated well. It must be backed by fair, objective performance measurement systems (like MBO or BARS) to be credible and effective.

10 marksExplain the salient features of the Minimum Wages Act, 1948. Include employer obligations, penalties, and significance.+

Introduction: The Minimum Wages Act, 1948 is one of the most important labour welfare legislations in India. It empowers the government to fix and revise minimum wages for workers in scheduled employments, thereby protecting them from exploitation and ensuring a minimum standard of living.

Definition: The Minimum Wages Act, 1948 is a law that mandates the Central and State governments to fix minimum wages for workers employed in scheduled (notified) industries and occupations, below which no employer can legally pay.

Salient Features:

1. Fixation of Minimum Wages: Both Central (for central sphere) and State (for state sphere) governments have the power to fix minimum wages. Wages are fixed for different categories of workers — skilled, semi-skilled, unskilled, and highly skilled.

2. Scheduled Employment: The Act applies to scheduled employments listed in the Schedule — including agriculture, construction, mining, bidi making, domestic work, road construction, and any other employment notified by government.

3. Components: Minimum wage consists of a basic rate of wages and a special allowance (cost of living/dearness allowance) linked to the Consumer Price Index (CPI) to protect against inflation.

4. Revision: Minimum wages must be reviewed and revised at least once in every 5 years. Dearness allowance may be revised more frequently (every 6 months) based on CPI changes.

5. Working Hours: The Act empowers government to fix normal working hours at 9 hours per day (with 1-hour rest) and 48 hours per week.

6. Overtime: Work beyond 9 hours per day or 48 hours per week must be compensated at double the minimum wage rate (2× overtime).

7. Employer's Obligations: No employer may pay below the fixed minimum wage. Wages must be paid on time. Wage records must be maintained. Display the minimum wage rates at the workplace.

8. Inspectors: Government appoints Inspectors with power to enter premises, examine wage records, and take evidence to verify compliance.

9. Penalties for Violation: Employer who pays below minimum wage faces: (a) imprisonment up to 6 months, or (b) fine up to ₹500, or (c) both. Repeat offences attract higher penalties.

10. Claims: Workers can file claims before the Authority within 6 months of the violation. If claim is proved, worker receives the unpaid difference plus compensation up to 10 times the shortfall.

Significance: The Act has been crucial in eliminating the exploitation of unorganised sector workers (domestic workers, agricultural labourers, construction workers) who lack bargaining power. It forms the foundation of India's wage policy.

Conclusion: The Minimum Wages Act, 1948 is a vital pillar of India's social security framework. Combined with the Code on Wages, 2019 (which merges this Act with three others), it continues to protect the rights of millions of Indian workers.
