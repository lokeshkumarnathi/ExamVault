  • Orienting and training employees
  • Setting performance targets
  • Conducting performance appraisals

13. HR Scorecard

Topic 13
HR Scorecard: A measurement tool that links HR activities and outcomes to the organization's strategic goals using key performance indicators (KPIs) — adapted from the Balanced Scorecard (BSC) by Kaplan & Norton.

Origin — Balanced Scorecard (BSC)

Developed by Kaplan & Norton (1992) for measuring overall business performance from four perspectives:

  • Financial — "How do we look to shareholders?"
  • Customer — "How do customers see us?"
  • Internal Process — "What must we excel at?"
  • Learning & Growth — "How can we improve?"

HR Scorecard — 4 Perspectives Applied to HR

PerspectiveHR Metric Example
FinancialCost per hire, Training ROI, Revenue per employee
Customer (Internal)Employee satisfaction score, manager satisfaction with HR
Internal ProcessTime to fill vacancy, onboarding cycle time
Learning & GrowthTraining hours per employee, % of succession plan filled

Benefits of HR Scorecard

  • Links HR to business strategy
  • Makes HR contribution measurable
  • Helps identify gaps in HR performance
  • Supports data-driven decisions
  • Justifies HR investments to top management

Steps to Build an HR Scorecard

  1. Understand organizational strategy
  2. Identify critical HR deliverables
  3. Create HR system map (activities → outcomes)
  4. Design HR metrics for each deliverable
  5. Implement measurement and feedback system

5-Mark Questions (Explain / Discuss)

Differentiate between HRM and Personnel Management. 5 marks
HRM vs. Personnel Management

Personnel Management (PM) is the traditional, administrative approach to managing employees, while HRM is the modern, strategic approach. Key differences:

1. Nature: PM is reactive and administrative; HRM is proactive and strategic.
2. Employee view: PM treats employees as a cost; HRM treats them as assets and human capital.
3. Focus: PM focuses on compliance and control; HRM focuses on employee development and engagement.
4. Time horizon: PM is short-term; HRM is long-term.
5. Integration: PM is a separate department; HRM is integrated with overall business strategy.
6. Communication: PM is indirect, top-down; HRM encourages direct, open communication.

Example: A PM approach would focus on keeping attendance records. An HRM approach would analyze attendance patterns and design flexible work policies to improve engagement.
Explain Dave Ulrich's HR Role Model. 5 marks
Dave Ulrich's 4-Role Model of HR

Dave Ulrich proposed that HR professionals must play four key roles to add value:

1. Strategic Partner: Aligns HR strategies with business goals. Works with senior management on long-term planning. Example: Creating a talent pipeline for the company's 5-year expansion plan.

2. Administrative Expert: Ensures HR processes are efficient and cost-effective. Manages payroll, compliance, and routine HR administration. Example: Digitizing leave management system.

3. Employee Champion: Listens to employees, addresses their concerns, and ensures their well-being and motivation. Example: Conducting employee engagement surveys and acting on feedback.

4. Change Agent: Helps the organization adapt to change by managing transformation programs. Example: Leading the transition to remote work during COVID-19.

These four roles are often shown in a 2×2 matrix with axes: Future/Strategic vs. Day-to-day/Operational, and People vs. Process.
Explain the concept of High Performance Work Systems (HPWS). 5 marks
High Performance Work Systems (HPWS)

HPWS is a set of integrated HR practices designed to enhance employee performance and organizational effectiveness. It is based on the AMO theory: employees perform well when they have the Ability, Motivation, and Opportunity.

Key Practices of HPWS:
1. Selective Hiring: Rigorous selection to hire the most capable candidates.
2. Extensive Training: Continuous skill development programs.
3. Performance-based Pay: Bonuses and incentives linked to results.
4. Employee Empowerment: Giving employees authority to make decisions.
5. Open Information Sharing: Transparency in organizational goals and performance.
6. Team-based Work: Use of self-managed or cross-functional teams.

Benefits: Higher productivity, lower turnover, better quality, innovation, and employee satisfaction.

Example: Google is a classic HPWS example — rigorous hiring (only top 0.5% hired), 20% project time (empowerment), generous benefits (motivation), and transparent OKR system (open information).
What is the POSH Act 2013? Explain its significance for EEO. 5 marks
POSH Act 2013 — Prevention of Sexual Harassment at Workplace

The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013, commonly known as the POSH Act, is a landmark Indian law to protect women from sexual harassment at work.

Key Provisions:
1. Applies to all workplaces — private, public, organized and unorganized sectors.
2. Every organization with 10+ employees must have an Internal Complaints Committee (ICC).
3. ICC must have at least 50% women members and an external member from an NGO.
4. Complaints must be resolved within 90 days.
5. Employers must conduct annual awareness training.
6. Penalties for non-compliance include fines and cancellation of business license.

Significance for EEO: POSH Act is a critical tool for ensuring Equal Employment Opportunity by creating a safe and dignified work environment for women, which is essential for them to participate equally in the workforce.

Example: Wipro, TCS, and all major Indian companies have ICC committees and mandatory POSH training as part of their HR compliance programs.

Explain the major Labour Laws in India and their importance. 10 marks
Introduction: Labour legislation in India consists of laws enacted to regulate the employer-employee relationship, protect workers' rights, ensure fair wages, and maintain industrial peace. India has one of the most comprehensive frameworks of labour law globally.

Major Labour Laws:

1. The Factories Act, 1948: Regulates health, safety, and welfare in factories. Limits working hours to 9 hours/day and 48 hours/week. Mandates proper ventilation, sanitation, and safety measures.

2. The Industrial Disputes Act, 1947: Regulates strikes, lockouts, layoffs, and retrenchment. Requires prior government permission for layoffs in companies with 100+ workers. Provides for labour courts and tribunals.

3. Minimum Wages Act, 1948: Empowers the government to fix minimum wages for different categories of work. Ensures no worker is exploited by paying below subsistence wages.

4. Payment of Wages Act, 1936: Ensures timely payment of wages. No unauthorized deductions allowed. Wages must be paid by the 7th or 10th of the following month.

5. Employees' Provident Fund Act, 1952: Mandatory for organizations with 20+ employees. Both employer and employee contribute 12% of basic salary. Provides retirement and social security benefits.

6. Payment of Gratuity Act, 1972: Applicable after 5 years of continuous service. Calculated as 15 days' salary per year of service. Maximum gratuity is ₹20 lakhs.

7. Employees' State Insurance Act, 1948: Covers employees earning up to ₹21,000/month. Provides medical benefits, maternity benefits, and disability benefits. Funded by employer (3.25%) and employee (0.75%) contributions.

8. Maternity Benefit Act, 1961 (amended 2017): Provides 26 weeks paid maternity leave for first two children. Mandatory crèche facility for establishments with 50+ employees.

9. Payment of Bonus Act, 1965: Minimum bonus = 8.33% of salary; Maximum = 20%. Applicable to employees earning up to ₹21,000/month.

10. Equal Remuneration Act, 1976: Equal pay for equal work regardless of gender. Prohibits gender discrimination in recruitment and promotion.

Recent Reform — Four Labour Codes (2020): The government consolidated 29 central labour laws into 4 codes: Code on Wages, Industrial Relations Code, Social Security Code, and Occupational Safety Code. This simplifies compliance and improves ease of doing business.

Conclusion: Labour laws in India create a framework of rights and obligations that protect workers while also providing a predictable environment for employers. Compliance with these laws is a critical HR responsibility.
What is HRIS? Explain its components, types, advantages and disadvantages. 10 marks
Introduction: HRIS (Human Resource Information System) is an integrated software solution that enables an organization to manage its human resources data electronically. It is the digital backbone of modern HRM.

Definition: HRIS is a system used to acquire, store, retrieve, and distribute information about an organization's human resources. It automates routine HR tasks while enabling data-driven strategic decisions.

Components of HRIS:
1. Employee Database: Personal information, employment history, skills, qualifications
2. Payroll Module: Salary processing, tax deductions, pay slip generation
3. Recruitment Module (ATS): Job postings, application tracking, interview scheduling
4. Training Module (LMS): Course catalog, training schedules, completion tracking
5. Performance Management Module: Goal setting, appraisal forms, 360-degree feedback
6. Leave Management: Leave applications, approvals, balance tracking
7. Analytics & Reporting: HR dashboards, compliance reports, workforce analytics

Types of HRIS:
1. Operational HRIS: Supports day-to-day record keeping and transactions
2. Tactical HRIS: Supports mid-level HR decisions like recruitment planning
3. Strategic HRIS: Supports strategic decisions like workforce planning and succession

Advantages of HRIS:
1. Eliminates paperwork and reduces administrative burden
2. Fast and accurate data retrieval
3. Real-time reporting and dashboards
4. Improved data accuracy — reduces human error
5. Employee and manager self-service portals
6. Better regulatory compliance and audit trails
7. Enables people analytics for strategic decisions

Disadvantages of HRIS:
1. High initial implementation costs
2. Data security and privacy risks
3. Requires significant training for users
4. Risk of system downtime affecting operations
5. Resistance to change from employees and managers
6. Ongoing maintenance and upgrade costs

Popular HRIS in India: SAP SuccessFactors, Darwinbox, Keka HR, Zoho People, Workday

Conclusion: HRIS has transformed HRM from a paper-based, reactive function to a digital, proactive, and strategic one. In today's competitive environment, organizations that leverage HRIS effectively gain significant advantages in talent management and decision-making.

Quick Revision — All 14 Topics

1. Introduction to HRM

HRM = Recruit + Select + Train + Appraise + Compensate + Maintain employees for organizational goals. HRM vs PM: HRM is strategic, proactive, views employees as assets. PM is reactive, administrative, views employees as costs. Functions: Managerial (PODC) + Operative (RSTPCE).

2. Line Managers

Direct supervisors who implement HR policies. They select, orient, train, appraise, and discipline their own team. Key point: HR makes policy, line managers implement it. Line = direct authority. Staff = advisory role.

3. HR Roles (Dave Ulrich)

4 Roles: Strategic Partner (align HR to strategy) + Administrative Expert (efficient processes) + Employee Champion (advocate for people) + Change Agent (drive transformation). 2×2 matrix: Future-People-Process-Day-to-day.

4. New HR Organization

Three-legged stool: SSC (Shared Service Centers — routine tasks) + COE (Centers of Excellence — specialists) + HRBP (Business Partners — strategic). Also: outsourcing, e-HR, Agile HR.

5. Globalization Trends

HR must manage multicultural teams, expatriates, cross-cultural training. EPG Model: Ethnocentric (home country staff), Polycentric (host country staff), Geocentric (best person globally). Example: TCS, Infosys in 50+ countries.

6. Technology Trends

AI (resume screening), Big Data (turnover prediction), Cloud (remote HR access), Social Media (LinkedIn hiring), Blockchain (credential verification), Gamification (training). Challenges: data privacy, cost, resistance, digital divide.

7. Nature of Work Trends

Gig economy, Remote work, Hybrid work, Automation, Knowledge work. Worker types: Full-time, Part-time, Contingent/Gig, Outsourced. HR challenge: managing performance virtually, legal classification of gig workers.

8. Workforce & Demographics

4 generations: Boomers (1946–64, loyal), Gen X (1965–80, independent), Millennials (1981–96, purpose-driven), Gen Z (1997–2012, flexible). Trends: aging workforce, more women, rising education. Strategy: diversity training, inclusive hiring, flexible work.

9. Economic Challenges

Recession → layoffs, VRS, pay cuts. Growth → aggressive hiring, retention programs. Inflation → compensation review. Labour shortage → employer branding, upskilling. India example: COVID-19 impact — remote work, pay cuts; Post-COVID: talent wars.

10. HPWS

AMO Model: Ability (training, selection) + Motivation (pay, recognition) + Opportunity (empowerment, participation). Key practices: selective hiring, extensive training, performance pay, self-managed teams, open information. Example: Google's HR = HPWS.

11. Labour Legislation India

Key laws: Factories Act (1948), ID Act (1947), Min Wages (1948), EPF (1952), Gratuity (1972), ESI (1948), Maternity (1961/2017 — 26 weeks), Bonus (1965), Equal Remuneration (1976). New reform: 29 laws → 4 Labour Codes (2020).

12. Equal Employment Opportunity

No discrimination based on gender, caste, religion, disability, age. Types: Disparate Treatment (intentional) vs Disparate Impact (neutral policy that harms a group). India laws: Articles 14/15/16, Reservation policy, POSH Act 2013 (ICC committee, 90-day resolution), Equal Remuneration Act 1976.

13. HR Scorecard

Based on Kaplan & Norton's Balanced Scorecard (1992). 4 perspectives: Financial (cost per hire), Customer (employee satisfaction), Process (time to fill), Learning & Growth (training hours). Purpose: link HR to strategy, measure HR contribution with KPIs.

14. HRIS

Digital system to manage HR data. Functions: Employee records, Payroll, Recruitment (ATS), Training (LMS), Performance, Leave, Analytics. Types: Operational, Tactical, Strategic. Software: SAP SuccessFactors, Darwinbox, Keka, Workday. Pros: reduces paperwork, fast retrieval. Cons: costly, security risk, resistance.
